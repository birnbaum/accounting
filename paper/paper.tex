\section{Background: \SCI and the Sunk Carbon Fallacy}
\label{sec:background}

The community's per-workload carbon metric is the \emph{Software Carbon Intensity} (\SCI),
codified by the Green Software Foundation and adopted as ISO/IEC 21031~\cite{sci-spec}:
\[
\SCI \;=\; \frac{E \cdot I \;+\; M}{R},
\]
where $E$ is the energy consumed running the workload, $I$ the carbon intensity of that
energy, $M$ the amortised embodied carbon of the server, and $R$ a unit of work
(API call, transaction, training run, $\ldots$). The metric is real-time-sensitive,
applicable from a single function to a datacenter, and intended to drive engineering
optimisation.

Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that \SCI contains a structural bug they
call the \emph{sunk-carbon fallacy}: because $M$ is amortised at a flat rate over the
device's expected lifespan, the per-unit-work $M$ does not respond to declining
efficiency, so as hardware ages, $E$ grows but $M$/$R$ does not, and a carbon-aware
scheduler optimising \SCI is biased toward older, less efficient
servers --- the opposite of what total-footprint minimisation requires. Provider
amortisation conventions (AWS/Azure 6\,yr, GCP 4\,yr; all zero out past EL ---
Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3}) sharpen this into the limit case where past-EL hardware
contributes $M=0$ per request; we note that the \SCI specification itself is silent on
$\text{TiR}>\text{EL}$, so the $M=0$ reading is a provider operationalisation rather
than an \SCI mandate, but it is the reading customers see today. To fix the underlying
fallacy, Bashir et al.\ introduce two refinements:
\[
\OSCI = \frac{E \cdot I}{R}, \qquad
\TSCI = \frac{tO + tM}{R},
\]
where \OSCI drops the embodied term entirely, and \TSCI keeps it but
allocates the \emph{entire datacenter's} operational and embodied carbon (active and
idle servers alike) across jobs by time, idle, and usage fractions. Both avoid the
sunk-carbon fallacy --- the per-job number no longer depends on the specific server it
lands on. \OSCI is cheap to compute; \TSCI is sound but demands
datacenter-wide telemetry that public-cloud customers cannot
access~\cite[\S3.1]{bashir2024}.

\OSCI resolves the sunk-carbon fallacy by dropping embodied carbon entirely,
but this is not free. Without an embodied term, \OSCI (i) does not reward
frugal hardware reservation beyond what energy savings already imply; (ii) gives no
signal to prefer hardware procured with low-embodied processes; and (iii) does not
disincentivize peak-hour use --- the lever that delays new hardware purchases in
capacity-constrained datacenters. \TSCI would restore these incentives by
construction, but, as Bashir notes, it requires comprehensive datacenter-level
information that public-cloud customers cannot
access~\cite[\S3.1]{bashir2024}.\footnote{The \TSCI ambition --- allocating the full
datacenter footprint to individual jobs --- is closely related to ours, but tackled
from the opposite direction. We return to this parallel in \S\ref{sec:future-work}.}

There is a second, structurally distinct weakness shared by every variant in the
\SCI family: none of them connects to the cloud provider's customer-allocated
GHG report. An engineering team that improves \SCI, \OSCI, or
\TSCI cannot point to a matching change in the company's corporate
sustainability disclosure. A metric whose improvement does not land in audited
reporting will not be funded, sustained, or trusted at scale --- and the cloud
provider's number does not move in response to bottom-up optimisation. The
decoupling is not merely theoretical: Jacquet et al.~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy}
demonstrate empirically that current per-VM provider attribution can
under-estimate operational CO$_2$ by up to 3$\times$ for high-utilisation
workloads on production OVHcloud servers, because static quantity-based
allocation misses the logarithmic load-dependence of server efficiency. The
next section grounds this second critique in what providers actually publish
today.

\paragraph{Three non-summable methodology families.}
The Tier-A tools differ in what they take as the \emph{unit of allocation}, and the
three resulting families are not directly comparable.
\emph{Usage-time allocated} (AWS, GCP, Azure, Oracle, IBM, Alibaba) treats the
\emph{rental-line} as the proxy: carbon is proportional to vCPU-hours or instance-hours
of an SKU, possibly re-weighted by list price (GCP), revenue (AWS non-foundational),
or a ``normalized cost'' metric (Azure). Embodied carbon is folded into the per-SKU
factor and amortised flat.
\emph{Traffic-allocated} (Akamai, Cloudflare, Fastly) takes the \emph{work-volume} as
the proxy: bytes delivered or CPU-time consumed; embodied hardware is largely or
entirely excluded.
\emph{Component-LCA per resource} (OVHcloud, Scaleway) builds the number from a
\emph{physical inventory}: every reserved component (rack, cores, GB~RAM, TB~SSD)
contributes a published LCA factor, plus an operational energy term.
The three families allocate different slices of provider emissions in different units;
adding or directly benchmarking numbers across them is not meaningful without an
explicit residual bridge --- precisely the gap \S\ref{sec:rsci} formalises.

\paragraph{Non-provider alternatives.}
Two open / academic efforts sit alongside this landscape and reach a similar
verdict from the bottom-up side. BoaviztAPI~\cite{simon2024boaviztapi} is an
open-source provider-agnostic toolkit that assembles per-component LCA
factors (CPU, RAM, SSD, building / technical environment) into per-instance
multi-criteria impacts (GWP, ADP, PE); their own Table~1 independently
documents the same provider-methodology divergence we report in
Table~\ref{tab:tier-a} and Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3}. Cinergy~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy},
developed in partnership with OVHcloud and validated across six server
architectures, produces deterministic per-VM power estimates and reports the
3$\times$ under-attribution finding cited in \S\ref{sec:background}. Both
remain bottom-up: neither reconciles to the provider's top-down report,
so both fall under the \S\ref{sec:background} critique. Their per-component
($\varepsilon_p$) factors are the natural bottom-up engine that feeds
\RSCI (\S\ref{sec:rsci}).

\section{\RSCI: Reconciling Bottom-Up and Top-Down}
\label{sec:rsci}

% NOTE: framing wrapper depends on §5 experiments. Math is committed; opening list
% of "four gaps closed" follows the §2 critique structure.

We introduce \emph{Reconcilable \SCI} (\RSCI), a class of per-workload metrics designed
to close four gaps identified in \S\ref{sec:background} and \S\ref{sec:sota}:
(i) the sunk-carbon fallacy of \SCI;
(ii) the loss of embodied-carbon incentives in \OSCI;
(iii) the absence of a peak-shaving / hardware-procurement signal in \OSCI;
(iv) the disconnect between every \SCI variant and the cloud provider's
top-down carbon report.
\RSCI does so by anchoring a bottom-up energy model to the provider's per-scope
top-down report via residual bridges, and by allocating each residual component
across workloads with a weight chosen to match its physical driver. The choice of
weight is itself a design surface --- the central vision contribution of this
paper.

\paragraph{Setup.}
Let $i$ index \emph{workload instances} --- one evaluation of a workload over period
$t_i$. The provider issues a top-down report broken into Scope-1, Scope-2, and
Scope-3 totals at the level of a \emph{reporting slice} indexed by service $s$ and
region $r$ (the finest granularity providers publish: per-month, per-project, per-service).
Let $C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S1}}$, $C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S2}}$, and
$C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S3}}$ denote the reported per-scope emissions of slice $(s,r)$.

\paragraph{Bottom-up energy.}
For workload $i$ in slice $(s,r)$ we estimate energy from per-resource quantities:
\begin{equation}
E_{i,s,r} \;=\; \sum_{p} q_p \cdot \varepsilon_p \quad [\text{Wh}],
\label{eq:bu-energy}
\end{equation}
where $p$ indexes resource types (vCPU-hours, GPU-hours, GB-hours of storage, I/O ops,
network GB) and $\varepsilon_p$ is a per-type energy coefficient. Concrete
instantiations of $\varepsilon_p$ are available from open-data projects such as
BoaviztAPI~\cite{simon2024boaviztapi} (per-component LCA factors and CPU power
curves) and per-VM deterministic models such as Cinergy~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy}
(production-validated on OVHcloud); \RSCI is orthogonal to the choice of
bottom-up engine. Unobservable resource types simply enlarge the residuals
defined below.

\paragraph{Residual bridges.}
For each top-down component $k$ reported by the provider, the residual is the gap
between what the provider reports and what the bottom-up model accounts for:
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{k}_{s,r} \;=\; C^{\downarrow k}_{s,r} \;-\; O^{k}_{s,r},
\label{eq:residual-general}
\end{equation}
where $O^{k}_{s,r}$ is the bottom-up estimate of that same quantity (zero for
components the bottom-up model does not address). At the coarsest decomposition ---
one residual per scope ---
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\text{S1}}_{s,r} = C^{\downarrow\text{S1}}_{s,r}, \;\;
\Delta^{\text{S2}}_{s,r} = C^{\downarrow\text{S2}}_{s,r} - \!\sum_i\! E_{i,s,r} I_r, \;\;
\Delta^{\text{S3}}_{s,r} = C^{\downarrow\text{S3}}_{s,r},
\label{eq:residual-scopes}
\end{equation}
with $I_r$ the location-based grid intensity of region $r$.
$\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ absorbs diesel and refrigerant emissions; $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$
absorbs PUE, idle capacity, market-vs-location adjustments, and energy-model error;
$\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ absorbs embodied hardware, building embodied, FERA, upstream
transport, and the other categories of Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3}. Where the
provider reports finer-grained sub-categories --- and where the operator finds it
useful --- the same definition (Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-general}) admits further
splitting; we will use a minimal split of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ into FERA, embodied IT,
and non-IT (building, freight, end-of-life) below.

\paragraph{Definition.}
Reconciliation is performed per slice $(s,r)$. Each residual component $k$ is
allocated across workloads by a weight $w^{k}_{i,s,r}$ satisfying
$\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r} = 1$, and the per-workload metric is
\begin{equation}
\RSCI_i \;=\; \frac{1}{R_i}\sum_{s,r} \Bigl[\, E_{i,s,r}\cdot I_r \;+\; \sum_{k} w^{k}_{i,s,r}\cdot\Delta^{k}_{s,r} \,\Bigr].
\label{eq:rsci}
\end{equation}
The first inner term is the bottom-up operational footprint; the second is the
workload's share of each residual component under the chosen weight. The choice of
$w^{k}$ is left open by Eq.~\ref{eq:rsci}: different weight families yield metrics
that incentivise different operator behavior, while preserving reconciliation by
construction (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}). The next subsection makes this design surface
explicit.

\subsection{Weight families: the residual as a design surface}
\label{sec:rsci-design}

Three weight families cover the residual components today's provider reports
expose, each motivated by a different physical driver of the residual.

\paragraph{Energy-share weights.}
\begin{equation}
w^{k}_{i,s,r} \;=\; \frac{E_{i,s,r}}{E_{s,r}}, \qquad E_{s,r} = \sum_i E_{i,s,r}.
\label{eq:w-energy}
\end{equation}
Appropriate when the physical driver of $\Delta^{k}$ is electricity drawn: the
load-proportional portion of $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ (PUE overhead, MBM adjustments,
energy-model error), and the FERA sub-component of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ (upstream
fuel and T\&D losses are physically proportional to kWh consumed).

\paragraph{Static-share weights.}
\begin{equation}
w^{k}_{i,s,r} \;=\; \frac{1}{M_{s,r}},
\label{eq:w-static}
\end{equation}
where $M_{s,r}$ is the number of active workloads in the slice. Appropriate when the
driver is facility uptime or facility existence --- costs incurred by being ``in
the room'' rather than by consumption. Sharma and Fuerst's accountable-FaaS
framework~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless} adopts this rule for
idle power and for embodied carbon under a single-server abstraction, on the
principle (their \S5) that embodied is more naturally treated as a \emph{static sunk
cost} than a usage-based cost. We adopt the same rule for $\Delta^{\text{S1}}$
(diesel backup, refrigerant leakage) and for the non-IT sub-components of
$\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ (building embodied, freight, end-of-life).

\paragraph{Peak-attribution weights.}
\begin{equation}
w^{k}_{i,s,r} \;=\; \frac{\varphi_i}{\sum_j \varphi_j},
\label{eq:w-peak}
\end{equation}
where $\varphi_i$ is workload $i$'s contribution to fleet peak --- formally, the
Shapley value of $i$ in the cooperative game whose value function is the slice's
peak provisioned capacity. Appropriate for the embodied-IT sub-component of
$\Delta^{\text{S3}}$, whose physical driver is peak provisioning: the workload at
peak forces the next server purchase; the workload at the trough does not. Exact
Shapley over the peak-capacity game is exponential in workload count and infeasible
online; following the precedent set
by~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless}, we treat $\varphi_i$ as a
\emph{best-effort Shapley approximation} satisfying the four Shapley properties
(efficiency, null-player, symmetry, linearity). Practical proxies that retain the
qualitative gradient include:
(a)~\emph{peak-hour energy share} $\varphi_i = E_i(t^{*})$ with $t^{*}$ the slice
peak hour;
(b)~\emph{leave-one-out marginals} $\varphi_i = \text{peak}(\mathcal{N}) -
\text{peak}(\mathcal{N}\setminus\{i\})$;
(c)~\emph{sampled-permutation} Shapley over a few hundred coalition orderings.
The choice of proxy is itself a design surface; \RSCI reconciles under any choice
satisfying $\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r} = 1$.

\paragraph{Default mapping.}
Mapping the three families to the residual components of
Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-scopes} (and its finer Scope-3 split) yields a default schema:
energy-share for $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ and $\Delta^{\text{S3-FERA}}$; static-share
for $\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ and the non-IT sub-components of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$;
peak-attribution for $\Delta^{\text{S3-embodied-IT}}$. This is a starting point, not
a prescription: operators with finer telemetry can split residual components further
or substitute other weights, and \RSCI reconciles as long as each chosen weight
family sums to one across workloads in the slice. The vision contribution of this
paper is the \emph{recognition that the residual is a design surface for
sustainable-behavior incentives}, not a fixed allocation rule.

\paragraph{Reconciliation by construction.}
Because $\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r} = 1$ for every component $k$, summing
$\RSCI_i \cdot R_i$ across all workloads in a slice yields the top-down
per-scope total:
\begin{equation}
\sum_i \RSCI_i \cdot R_i \;=\; \sum_{s,r}\Bigl(C^{\downarrow\text{S1}}_{s,r} + C^{\downarrow\text{S2}}_{s,r} + C^{\downarrow\text{S3}}_{s,r}\Bigr).
\label{eq:reconcile}
\end{equation}
Reconciliation is not an empirical property to verify; it is a structural identity
that holds under \emph{any} admissible weight choice for \emph{any} residual
component.

\paragraph{Properties.}
Under the default mapping, \RSCI closes the four gaps from the section opening as
direct consequences of the chosen weights.
(i) \emph{Sunk-carbon}: \RSCI has no per-server $M$ term. Embodied carbon lives in
$\Delta^{\text{S3-embodied-IT}}$ and is allocated by the peak-attribution weight
(Eq.~\ref{eq:w-peak}). A workload on older, less efficient hardware draws more
energy through $E_{i,s,r}\cdot I_r$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:rsci} and reports a higher \RSCI
regardless of how embodied is amortised on the provider's books --- the per-request
operational gradient carries the sunk-carbon-avoidance argument on its own.
(ii) \emph{Embodied incentive}: under static-share or peak-attribution weights for
the embodied component, workloads sized to fit actual demand pay less embodied than
over-reserved ones --- a signal \OSCI structurally cannot supply.
(iii) \emph{Peak-shaving and procurement deferral}: under peak-attribution weights
(Eq.~\ref{eq:w-peak}), a workload running at the slice peak draws a larger embodied
share than one running at the trough, \emph{even at identical energy}. This is the
incentive structure operators need to defer fleet expansion, and it is a property no
bottom-up metric (\SCI, \OSCI, \TSCI) can supply because the design surface --- a
residual to allocate --- does not exist without top-down anchoring.
(iv) \emph{Reconciliation}: Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}.

\paragraph{Negative residuals are admissible.}
Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-general} does not constrain the sign of $\Delta^{k}_{s,r}$.
When the bottom-up estimate $O^{k}_{s,r}$ exceeds the provider's reported
$C^{\downarrow k}_{s,r}$ for a component, the residual is negative and the
corresponding term in Eq.~\ref{eq:rsci} \emph{reduces} the workload's
attributed footprint. Empirically this case arises: Jacquet et
al.~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy} show that current provider attribution can
under-estimate operational CO$_2$ by up to 3$\times$ for high-utilisation VMs.
\RSCI absorbs such cases by construction --- reconciliation
(Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}) still holds --- and a negative $\Delta^{k}_{s,r}$ is
itself a diagnostic: it signals that the provider's top-down report
under-counts what a credible bottom-up model attributes to the slice. The
metric captures the top-down report; whether the top-down report captures
physical truth is the provider's accountability, not the metric's.

\paragraph{Precision scales with data fidelity.}
\RSCI is a \emph{class} of metrics parameterised by the choice of weight family per
residual component, not a fixed equation. Its precision is bounded by three axes:
bottom-up model quality (coverage and accuracy of $\varepsilon_p$), top-down
reporting granularity (slice resolution + cadence), and the unit-of-work $R$. As
each improves, $\Delta$ shrinks and the residual-allocation choice matters less;
reconciliation (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}) holds at any level of data fidelity and
under any admissible weight choice. The next section confronts the theory with
today's provider reports.

\paragraph{Closing the loop.}

\RSCI is a sound bottom-up/top-down bridge in theory; it cannot be computed in
practice on today's provider reports because the top-down side is too coarse, too
delayed, and methodologically opaque. The next section identifies the specific
provider, estimation, and standards changes needed to close the gap.

\section{Closing the Gap: A Call to Action}
\label{sec:asks}

% TODO: revise after experiments. Working hypothesis: the three buckets below
% (providers / estimation / standards) are the right partition; specifics may shift
% based on which axis the experiments show is most bottle-necked.

If reconciliation-by-construction is structurally available but empirically
unrealised, the question is what would change that. We see three complementary
buckets.

\paragraph{Provider asks.}
Customer carbon must be reported at \emph{billing frequency} (hourly, resource-level)
to match the rate at which workloads change. Revenue and ``equivalent revenue''
fallbacks (AWS non-foundational; cf.\ Table~\ref{tab:tier-a} footnote~$a$) should be
replaced with physical-telemetry allocation. Customer-tool methodology must reconcile
with corporate disclosure --- Microsoft's open admission that the two do not match
(\S\ref{sec:sota}) is a contract violation against the customer's reporting need, not
a feature. Excluded Scope-3 categories require published materiality assessments, as
the GHG Protocol already mandates.

\paragraph{Estimation techniques.}
Even at current reporting cadence, three lines of estimation can compress the
residual: (a) cross-tenant pooling of $\hat\mu_{s,r}, \hat\rho_{s,r}$, since these
quantities are driven by provider methodology rather than individual customers;
(b) Kalman-filter state-estimation over reconciled-month sequences to track drift in
PUE and hardware refresh; (c) Shapley-value attribution (or tractable proxies) for
the embodied term to sharpen the peak-shaving incentive beyond plain energy-share
weighting. We do not require providers to expose proprietary telemetry --- only to
report often enough that customer-side estimation can converge.

\paragraph{Standards and policy.}
The GHG Protocol is under-specified for cloud~\cite[see][]{schneider-mattia-2024};
allocation of shared infrastructure, equipment lifetimes, EAC temporal matching, and
building embodied carbon are interpretive divergences arising from gaps in the
standard, not violations~\cite[see ``Under-Specified GHG Protocol Areas'']{bashir2024}.
A cloud-specific gold standard must close these and require independent assurance of
\emph{tool implementation}, not just the methodology document.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future-work}

% TODO: tighten after §5 lands. Current draft covers (i) signals beyond carbon,

The residual-decomposition framework is \emph{signal-agnostic}: replace
$C^{\downarrow}_{s,r}$ with a top-down water-use report and the same machinery yields
a ``water per unit of work'' metric reconcilable with the provider's water disclosure.
Scaleway and OVHcloud already publish water; abiotic-resource and waste signals could
follow. A unified attribution layer would let the community extend reconciliable
metrics to any sustainability dimension a provider chooses to report.

On the attribution side, the energy-share weighting in \S\ref{sec:rsci} is a
deliberately simple default that is only incentive-aligned for $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$.
Kalman filtering for $\hat\mu$ state-estimation and Shapley-value attribution
(or tractable proxies --- peak-hour share, leave-one-out marginals) for $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$
are the two concrete refinements we see most leverage in --- the latter sharpens the
peak-shaving / hardware-procurement incentive in a way energy-share weighting cannot,
and crucially avoids penalising clean-grid trough usage with an embodied burden it
did not cause.
\todo[inline]{Open theoretical question (chat 2026-05-17): once $\varphi_i$ is
estimated rather than computed exactly, reconciliation holds only in expectation.
Can we give a \emph{bounded-correlation} guarantee --- a worst-case bound on
$|\sum_i \widehat{\RSCI}_i R_i - C^{\downarrow}_{s,r}|$ as a function of
sample budget and fleet heterogeneity? This would convert the framework's headline
property from ``reconcilable by construction'' to ``provably $\varepsilon$-close
under estimated attribution,'' which is a more honest claim under practical
attribution and likely a paper-strengthener.}

Bashir's \TSCI and \RSCI share the goal of allocating the whole datacenter
footprint to individual jobs, but tackle it from opposite directions: \TSCI
aggregates bottom-up using datacenter telemetry only providers have; \RSCI inverts
the problem and absorbs the same overhead from the top-down report. Both are sound;
\RSCI is the version a customer can actually compute. A hybrid that uses \TSCI
on the provider side as the top-down anchor for \RSCI on the customer side is an
intriguing direction.

Multi-tenant and serverless settings reshape the residual decomposition in ways we
do not address here; how to allocate the residual across tenants sharing a single
warm pool is open. Finally, standardising the bottom-up energy coefficients
$\varepsilon_p$ so \RSCI is comparable across organisations is the remaining
sociotechnical step.
