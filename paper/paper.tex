\begin{abstract}
Quantifying the carbon footprint of cloud workloads has produced two largely separate responses.
Bottom-up metrics such as the Software Carbon Intensity (\SCI) provide real-time, actionable signals for energy-efficient and carbon-aware scheduling and optimization.
Meanwhile, cloud providers have begun publishing top-down corporate GHG reports and customer-facing dashboards that let tenants estimate their footprint from cloud usage.
Because the two approaches differ in scope and reporting frequency, the current state of the art treats them as decoupled: bottom-up metrics for optimization, top-down numbers for corporate disclosure.

We argue that although the two signals can never be identical, \emph{they must be reconcilable}. 
If teams cannot trace their bottom-up improvements through to the provider's top-down report, they will lose trust in both. 
We survey the state of the art in cloud carbon accounting, identify the gaps between the two approaches, and propose a vision for \RSCI: a metric that re-anchors a bottom-up energy estimate to the provider's per-scope report through a residual bridge that absorbs what the bottom-up model misses. 
\RSCI aligns the two signals by construction, captures all provider-side overheads, and degrades gracefully when a provider does not yet ship reconciliation-grade data.
The residual can itself be allocated to workloads in ways that incentivize sustainable behavior---for example, penalizing demand peaks that force new hardware procurement---which no bottom-up-only metric can do.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A major challenge in sustainable computing is correctly measuring and accounting for the carbon footprint of workloads. 
It is a prerequisite for determining the effectiveness of carbon-reduction strategies, for reporting to regulators and investors, and for making informed decisions about workload placement, scaling, and adaptation. 

Most research in so-called \emph{carbon-aware computing} has focused on optimizing operational carbon, so the emissions from a workload's marginal increase in energy consumption. 
This signal is straightforward to obtain (workload energy $\times$ carbon intensity) but does, for example, not capture the embodied carbon of the hardware running the workload.
To provide a more comprehensive metric, the Green Software Foundation proposed the Software Carbon Intensity (\SCI) specification~\cite{sci-spec}, codified as ISO/IEC~21031 in 2024, which expresses the carbon emitted per unit of work and folds in an embodied term.
However, Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that \SCI can produce perverse incentives for workload placement and scaling in practice. 
Furthermore, all bottom-up operational carbon metrics, \SCI
included, are by construction blind to provider-side overheads (idle power, cooling, the embodied carbon of the datacenter) which a cloud tenant or cluster user cannot even observe.

Motivated by an increase regulatory pressure~\cite{eu2022csrd,SEC2024_3311275,sbti2024netzero} for corporate sustainability reporting, major cloud providers have started to publish top-down GHG reports and customer-facing dashboards that allow tenants to estimate and report their carbon footprint from cloud usage.
They enable tenants to report their carbon footprint in a way that is consistent with the provider's corporate sustainability disclosure, which is critical for regulatory compliance and investor relations.
While the general methodology is usually public and sometimes even externally audited, the scope of the reported reports differ significantly by provider and underlying details of how reported numbers were calculated are not disclosed.
Moreover, the reports are usually published on a monthly basis, about 14-21 days after the end of each month, which makes them comprehensive but entirely unsuitable any type of real-time optimization.

A structural problem remains: there exists no bottom-up metric that connects to the cloud provider's customer-allocated GHG report.
An engineering team that improves any of these signals cannot point to a matching change in the company's corporate sustainability disclosure, because the report contains overheads no bottom-up energy model captures without provider-internal data: PUE, idle and over-provisioned capacity, market-based adjustments, upstream fuel-and-transmission losses, building embodied carbon, refrigerant leakage, water, and end-of-life.
The Green Software Foundation explicitly treats this gap as a feature, distinguishing ``measuring for action'' (per-workload signals that drive engineering) from ``measuring for reporting'' (organization-level inventories that satisfy regulators) and arguing that the two pursue different goals and should not be conflated~\cite{gsf-measuring}.
We disagree.
In an environment where regulators now require firm-level GHG disclosure~\cite{eu2022csrd,SEC2024_3311275,sbti2024netzero} and where carbon pricing is increasingly implemented, a per-workload metric whose improvement does not land in audited reporting will not be funded, sustained, or trusted at scale.
% Microsoft's own 2021 Cloud Supply Chain whitepaper concedes that its customer-tool methodology ``\emph{will differ from those reflected in Microsoft's corporate disclosure}''~\cite[p.~2, fn.~2]{microsoft-s3-2021} --- a gap this paper treats as the structural failure mode, not a design choice.

We argue that any per-workload sustainability metric intended to drive both engineering decisions and corporate disclosure should \emph{reconcile} with the provider's top-down GHG report by construction.
We introduce \emph{Reconcilable \SCI} (\RSCI) as a concrete metric in this class: it anchors a bottom-up energy estimate to the provider's per-scope top-down report through a \emph{residual bridge} for each Scope, so that per-workload numbers sum to the reported total as a structural identity rather than an empirical check (\S\ref{sec:rsci}).
\RSCI captures all provider-side overheads, because everything the bottom-up model misses is absorbed into the residual rather than dropped; it is structurally sunk-carbon-free, with embodied carbon living in the Scope-3 residual; and it degrades gracefully to $E \cdot I$ when the provider does not yet ship reconciliation-grade data.
Critically, reconciliation forces an explicit residual term per scope, and the choice of \emph{how} to allocate that residual across workloads becomes a design surface that determines what the metric incentivises --- including a peak-shaving and procurement-deferral incentive no bottom-up metric can supply (\S\ref{sec:rsci-design}).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item A structural critique of the \SCI family: every variant is a bottom-up
energy model decoupled by construction from the provider's top-down report and
therefore misses most of the carbon picture (\S\ref{sec:background}).
\item A cross-provider survey of carbon-accounting tooling through a
\emph{reconcilability} lens, including a lever-by-provider diagnostic matrix
showing which engineering actions actually move which provider's reported
number (\S\ref{sec:sota}, \S\ref{sec:practice}).
\item \RSCI: the only per-workload metric in the \SCI family that reconciles
with the provider's top-down report by construction, captures all provider-side
overheads, is structurally sunk-carbon-free, and degrades gracefully to \OSCI
under limited data (\S\ref{sec:rsci}).
\item The \emph{residual as a design surface}: a family of weight choices
(energy-share, static-share, peak-attribution) that preserve reconciliation by
construction and induce sharply different per-workload incentives ---

can supply (\S\ref{sec:rsci-design}; demonstrated in \S\ref{sec:demo}).
\item A diagnosis of why \RSCI cannot yet be computed on today's provider
reports, and a set of concrete asks on provider reporting, customer-side
estimation, and standards that would close the gap
(\S\ref{sec:practice}, \S\ref{sec:asks}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Bottom-Up Carbon Metrics}
\label{sec:background}

The community's per-workload carbon metric is the \emph{Software Carbon Intensity} (\SCI),
codified by the Green Software Foundation and adopted as ISO/IEC 21031~\cite{sci-spec}:
\[
\SCI \;=\; \frac{E \cdot I \;+\; M}{R},
\]
where $E$ is the energy consumed running the workload, $I$ the carbon intensity of that energy, $M$ the amortised embodied carbon of the server (the device's cradle-to-gate LCA total scaled by the request's share of its expected lifetime, following the architectural carbon-modelling convention of Gupta et al.'s ACT~\cite{gupta2022act}), and $R$ a unit of work (API call, transaction, training run, $\ldots$).
The metric is real-time-sensitive, applicable from a single function to a datacenter, and intended to drive engineering optimisation.

Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that \SCI contains a structural bug they call the \emph{sunk-carbon fallacy}: because $M$ is amortised at a flat rate over the device's expected lifespan, the per-unit-work $M$ does not respond to declining efficiency, so as hardware ages, $E$ grows but $M$/$R$ does not, and a carbon-aware scheduler optimising \SCI is biased toward older, less efficient servers --- the opposite of what total-footprint minimisation requires.
Provider amortisation conventions (AWS/Azure 6\,yr, GCP 4\,yr; all zero out past EL --- Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3}) sharpen this into the limit case where past-EL hardware contributes $M=0$ per request; we note that the \SCI specification itself is silent on $\text{TiR}>\text{EL}$, so the $M=0$ reading is a provider operationalisation rather than an \SCI mandate, but it is the reading customers see today.
To fix the underlying fallacy, Bashir et al.\ introduce two refinements:
\[
\OSCI = \frac{E \cdot I}{R}, \qquad
\TSCI = \frac{tO + tM}{R},
\]
where \OSCI drops the embodied term entirely, and \TSCI keeps it but allocates the \emph{entire datacenter's} operational and embodied carbon (active and idle servers alike) across jobs by time, idle, and usage fractions.
Both avoid the sunk-carbon fallacy --- the per-job number no longer depends on the specific server it lands on.
\OSCI is cheap to compute; \TSCI is sound but demands datacenter-wide telemetry that public-cloud customers cannot access~\cite[\S3.1]{bashir2024}.

\OSCI resolves the sunk-carbon fallacy by dropping embodied carbon entirely, but this is not free.
Without an embodied term, \OSCI (i) does not reward frugal hardware reservation beyond what energy savings already imply; (ii) gives no signal to prefer hardware procured with low-embodied processes; and (iii) does not disincentivize peak-hour use --- the lever that delays new hardware purchases in capacity-constrained datacenters.
\TSCI would restore these incentives by construction, but, as Bashir notes, it requires comprehensive datacenter-level information that public-cloud customers cannot access~\cite[\S3.1]{bashir2024}.\footnote{The \TSCI ambition --- allocating the full datacenter footprint to individual jobs --- is closely related to ours, but tackled from the opposite direction. We return to this parallel in \S\ref{sec:future-work}.}

There is a second, structurally distinct weakness shared by every variant in the \SCI family: none of them connects to the cloud provider's customer-allocated GHG report.
An engineering team that improves \SCI, \OSCI, or \TSCI cannot point to a matching change in the company's corporate sustainability disclosure.
A metric whose improvement does not land in audited reporting will not be funded, sustained, or trusted at scale --- and the cloud provider's number does not move in response to bottom-up optimisation.
The decoupling is not merely theoretical: Jacquet et al.~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy} demonstrate empirically that current per-VM provider attribution can under-estimate operational CO$_2$ by up to 3$\times$ for high-utilisation workloads on production OVHcloud servers, because static quantity-based allocation misses the logarithmic load-dependence of server efficiency.
The next section grounds this second critique in what providers actually publish today.

\section{State of the Art in Cloud Carbon Accounting}
\label{sec:sota}

\paragraph{Customer-facing tools are rare.}
We surveyed 48 cloud providers in May~2026, covering hyperscalers, mid-tier and regional IaaS, CDN/edge, AI-specialised neoclouds, application-PaaS, and sustainability-positioned regional operators (full enumeration with disclosure classification in Appendix~\ref{app:inventory}).
Only twelve ship a first-party, per-customer carbon dashboard --- the bar for a customer to source a GHG-Protocol-compliant Scope-3 Cat-1 input from their cloud spend.\footnote{We exclude marketplace third-party SaaS such as Huella Cloud on Huawei Cloud (vendor: SGS Solutions), since the per-provider allocation is not auditable against the provider's own emission factors. Exoscale's calculator is borderline (Exoscale-branded surface, CloudAssess engine by Resilio/Kleis) and is omitted.}
Of the remaining 36, fourteen publish corporate-level Scope~1/2/3 disclosures without any per-customer surface; the other 22 publish only marketing-grade renewable/PUE claims or nothing at all.
Most starkly, the fastest-growing segment --- AI/GPU clouds (CoreWeave, Lambda, Crusoe, Nebius, RunPod, Together, Replicate, Hyperstack, Verda, Fireworks~AI, Paperspace) --- has \emph{zero} per-customer tools.
Compute capacity for AI training and inference is being built at the fastest pace in cloud history with the \emph{least} customer carbon transparency.
The rest of this section focuses on the twelve per-customer tools.

\paragraph{One shared template, four axes of variation.}
Strip the marketing and every customer tool computes the same shape:
\[
\mathrm{customer\,carbon} \;=\;
\textstyle\sum_{r}\, u_{r}\,\cdot\,f_{r} \;+\; \mathrm{overheads},
\]
where $u_{r}$ is an activity unit (vCPU-hours, instance-hours, bytes, CPU-time, GB-month, \$-spend, server-hours) and $f_{r}$ is a per-unit factor.
Provider variation lives on four orthogonal axes: (A1)~which activity unit, (A2)~how $f_{r}$ is derived, (A3)~embodied carbon treatment, (A4)~idle/overhead allocation.
Figure~\ref{fig:axes} maps eleven of the twelve customer tools onto the two most discriminating axes (A1, A2); T Cloud Public is the twelfth (launched 2026-02-23) but has not disclosed its per-resource allocation rule.
The prior literature's three-family taxonomy (usage-time / traffic / component-LCA) puts AWS-foundational and OVHcloud-dedicated in different families even though both reduce to $\sum u_{r} f_{r}$, and we replace it with the per-axis decomposition below.

\paragraph{The ideal weighting is measured energy --- almost no one delivers it.}
The right question is not which usage unit a provider uses but how well that unit tracks the customer's actual energy draw.
For network, bytes are approximately linear in energy, so byte-share allocation is structurally sound (Cloudflare, Fastly-Delivery, Akamai's byte component).
For compute the relationship is highly non-linear --- an idle server still draws on the order of half its full-load power --- so vCPU-hours, instance-hours, and \$-spend systematically misweight idle and busy workloads.
Four customer-surface allocations approach energy-direct, in decreasing concreteness: \emph{Scaleway} combines per-component LCA with actual measured CPU consumption and published Boavizta consumption profiles for the monthly report, so an idle and a fully-loaded VM at the same SKU receive different allocations;\footnote{\url{https://www.scaleway.com/en/docs/environmental-footprint/}; per-product calculation pages compiled in our reference set.}
\emph{Akamai} meters kWh per server and per network device in its colocations\footnote{Akamai 2025 emissions-reporting policy and customer-reporting blog series.} and allocates DC-total energy by ``byte utilization and machine utilization'';
\emph{Oracle}'s power-based mode tracks kWh by service workload (LBM~+~MBM) but does not disclose the shared-hardware allocation algorithm;\footnote{\url{https://docs.oracle.com/en-us/iaas/releasenotes/governance/carbon-analysis-powercalculation.htm}, June~2025.}
and \emph{GCP-internal} measures power per machine via Borg telemetry but deliberately re-projects to vCPU-h~$\times$~list-price at the customer surface~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}.
The remaining compute-allocation tools (AWS, Azure, IBM, Alibaba, OVHcloud-currently) propagate a usage-unit proxy: an idle and a fully-loaded VM of the same SKU and duration produce the same number.
T Cloud Public claims a utilization-share allocation from measured device-level power but has not yet published its per-resource formula, so the customer-side weighting cannot be independently checked.

\paragraph{Coverage and verification are shallower than the dashboards suggest.}
Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3} breaks the Big-3 Scope-3 perimeter down by GHG-Protocol category.
No two providers cover the same set: AWS includes FERA and full Cat~2 capital goods (with buildings); GCP adds business travel and employee commuting but excludes end-of-life; Azure is the only Big-3 to include Cat~12 end-of-life yet excludes both FERA and building embodied carbon.
None publishes the materiality assessment the GHG Protocol requires to justify a category exclusion.
Headline numbers are therefore not directly comparable.
Third-party assurance is similarly shallow: AWS's Apex statement covers Scope-3 Cat~2/3/4 \emph{methodology} only, GCP's Fraunhofer review covers the LCA methodology only, Azure's WSP statement covers the Scope-3 \emph{document} only; \textbf{no Big-3 customer tool has been audited end-to-end}, and only Alibaba's Energy~Expert carries third-party \emph{data} assurance (Bureau Veritas, ISO~14064-3) of the per-customer numbers.
The temporal surface is similarly thin: \textbf{only Scaleway and Fastly publish daily customer-facing data}; the other nine, including all of the Big-3, are monthly; \emph{no} customer tool publishes hourly customer-allocated emissions, so any carbon-aware-scheduling claim today rests on signals outside the customer tool itself.

\paragraph{The internal/external gap is structural --- and is the \RSCI residual.}
Three providers say this out loud.
Microsoft's 2021 Scope-3 transparency whitepaper, attached to its own description of the customer-facing tool, states that ``\emph{the underlying methodologies and emissions findings generated from the calculator will differ from those reflected in Microsoft's corporate disclosure}''~\cite[p.~2, fn.~2]{microsoft-s3-2021}, and the January~2026 Azure methodology page repeats the point in operational terms: ``\emph{usage for emissions calculations might not match your Microsoft usage for billing purposes}''~\cite{azure-methodology-2026}.
GCP measures machine-level power via Borg hourly but re-projects to a monthly \emph{list-price-proportional} SKU factor at the customer surface, and Schneider \& Mattia note (\S3.6) that ``\emph{customers do not receive a signal about how switching from SKU~A to SKU~B can reduce their actual emissions, but rather their allocated emissions based on list prices}''~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}.
AWS's Model~3.0 defines a foundational/non-foundational service split but publishes neither the service list nor the share of emissions allocated by equivalent revenue~\cite{aws-ccft-model3}.
The pattern is not three isolated quirks: every customer tool ships a methodologically distinct, thinner subset of what the provider measures internally, and the size of that gap is the residual that \S\ref{sec:rsci} formalises through \RSCI.
Bottom-up engines such as BoaviztAPI~\cite{simon2024boaviztapi} and Cinergy~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy} reach the same verdict from the opposite side and are the natural feed for the bottom-up term in \RSCI; they do not themselves reconcile to the provider report.

\section{\RSCI: Reconciling Bottom-Up and Top-Down}
\label{sec:rsci}

% NOTE: framing wrapper depends on §5 experiments. Math is committed; opening list
% of "three gaps closed" follows the §2 critique structure.

We introduce \emph{Reconcilable \SCI} (\RSCI), a class of per-workload metrics designed
to close three gaps identified in \S\ref{sec:background} and \S\ref{sec:sota}:
(i) the sunk-carbon fallacy of \SCI;
(ii) the loss of embodied-carbon incentives in \OSCI --- and with them the
peak-shaving / hardware-procurement signal that embodied carbon should provide;
(iii) the disconnect between every \SCI variant and the cloud provider's
top-down carbon report.
\RSCI does so by anchoring a bottom-up energy model to the provider's per-scope
top-down report via residual bridges, and by allocating each residual component
across workloads with a weight chosen to match its physical driver. The choice of
weight is itself a design surface --- the central vision contribution of this
paper.

\paragraph{Setup.}
Let $i$ index \emph{workload instances} --- one evaluation of a workload over period
$t_i$. The provider issues a top-down report broken into Scope-1, Scope-2, and
Scope-3 totals at the level of a \emph{reporting slice} indexed by service $s$ and
region $r$ (the finest granularity providers publish: per-month, per-project, per-service).
Let $C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S1}}$, $C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S2}}$, and
$C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S3}}$ denote the reported per-scope emissions of slice $(s,r)$.

\paragraph{Bottom-up energy.}
For workload $i$ in slice $(s,r)$ we estimate energy from per-resource quantities:
\begin{equation}
E_{i,s,r} \;=\; \sum_{p} q_p \cdot \varepsilon_p \quad [\text{Wh}],
\label{eq:bu-energy}
\end{equation}
where $p$ indexes resource types (vCPU-hours, GPU-hours, GB-hours of storage, I/O ops, network GB) and $\varepsilon_p$ is a per-type energy coefficient.
Concrete instantiations of $\varepsilon_p$ are available from open-data projects such as BoaviztAPI~\cite{simon2024boaviztapi} (per-component LCA factors and CPU power curves) and per-VM deterministic models such as Cinergy~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy} (production-validated on OVHcloud); \RSCI is orthogonal to the choice of bottom-up engine.
Unobservable resource types simply enlarge the residuals defined below.

\paragraph{Residual bridges.}
For each top-down component $k$ reported by the provider, the residual is the gap between what the provider reports and what the bottom-up model accounts for:
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{k}_{s,r} \;=\; C^{\downarrow k}_{s,r} \;-\; O^{k}_{s,r},
\label{eq:residual-general}
\end{equation}
where $O^{k}_{s,r}$ is the bottom-up estimate of that same quantity (zero for components the bottom-up model does not address).
At the coarsest decomposition --- one residual per scope ---
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\text{S1}}_{s,r} = C^{\downarrow\text{S1}}_{s,r}, \;\;
\Delta^{\text{S2}}_{s,r} = C^{\downarrow\text{S2}}_{s,r} - \!\sum_i\! E_{i,s,r} I_r, \;\;
\Delta^{\text{S3}}_{s,r} = C^{\downarrow\text{S3}}_{s,r},
\label{eq:residual-scopes}
\end{equation}
with $I_r$ the location-based grid intensity of region $r$.
$\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ absorbs diesel and refrigerant emissions; $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ absorbs PUE, idle capacity, market-vs-location adjustments, and energy-model error; $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ absorbs embodied hardware, building embodied, FERA, upstream transport, and the other categories of Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3}.
Where the provider reports finer-grained sub-categories --- and where the operator finds it useful --- the same definition (Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-general}) admits further splitting; we will use a minimal split of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ into FERA, embodied IT, and non-IT (building, freight, end-of-life) below.

\paragraph{Definition.}
Reconciliation is performed per slice $(s,r)$.
Each residual component $k$ is allocated across workloads by a weight $w^{k}_{i,s,r}$ satisfying $\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r} = 1$, and the per-workload metric is
\begin{equation}
\RSCI_i \;=\; \frac{1}{R_i}\sum_{s,r} \Bigl[\, E_{i,s,r}\cdot I_r \;+\; \sum_{k} w^{k}_{i,s,r}\cdot\Delta^{k}_{s,r} \,\Bigr].
\label{eq:rsci}
\end{equation}
The first inner term is the bottom-up operational footprint; the second is the workload's share of each residual component under the chosen weight.
The choice of $w^{k}$ is left open by Eq.~\ref{eq:rsci}: different weight families yield metrics that incentivise different operator behavior, while preserving reconciliation by construction (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}).
The next subsection makes this design surface explicit.

\subsection{Weight families: the residual as a design surface}
\label{sec:rsci-design}

Three weight families cover the residual components today's provider reports
expose, each motivated by a different physical driver of the residual.

\paragraph{Energy-share weights.}
\begin{equation}
w^{k}_{i,s,r} \;=\; \frac{E_{i,s,r}}{E_{s,r}}, \qquad E_{s,r} = \sum_i E_{i,s,r}.
\label{eq:w-energy}
\end{equation}
Appropriate when the physical driver of $\Delta^{k}$ is electricity drawn: the load-proportional portion of $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ (PUE overhead, MBM adjustments, energy-model error), and the FERA sub-component of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ (upstream fuel and T\&D losses are physically proportional to kWh consumed).

\paragraph{Static-share weights.}
\begin{equation}
w^{k}_{i,s,r} \;=\; \frac{1}{M_{s,r}},
\label{eq:w-static}
\end{equation}
where $M_{s,r}$ is the number of active workloads in the slice.
Appropriate when the driver is facility uptime or facility existence --- costs incurred by being ``in the room'' rather than by consumption.
Sharma and Fuerst's accountable-FaaS framework~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless} adopts this rule for idle power and for embodied carbon under a single-server abstraction, on the principle (their \S5) that embodied is more naturally treated as a \emph{static sunk cost} than a usage-based cost.
We adopt the same rule for $\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ (diesel backup, refrigerant leakage) and for the non-IT sub-components of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ (building embodied, freight, end-of-life).

\paragraph{Peak-attribution weights.}
\begin{equation}
w^{k}_{i,s,r} \;=\; \frac{\varphi_i}{\sum_j \varphi_j},
\label{eq:w-peak}
\end{equation}
where $\varphi_i$ is workload $i$'s contribution to fleet peak --- formally, the Shapley value~\cite{shapley1953value} of $i$ in the cooperative game whose value function is the slice's peak provisioned capacity.
Appropriate for the embodied-IT sub-component of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$, whose physical driver is peak provisioning: the workload at peak forces the next server purchase; the workload at the trough does not.
The same physical argument was recently made for embodied-carbon attribution within a single server by Han et al.~\cite{HotCarbon2024GameTheoretic}, who frame ``\emph{embodied carbon attribution as the problem of demand-driven supply provisioning for hardware}''~\cite[\S3.4]{HotCarbon2024GameTheoretic}; \RSCI generalises the move to the slice / fleet scope via the top-down residual.
Exact Shapley over the peak-capacity game is exponential in workload count and infeasible online; following the precedent set by~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless,HotCarbon2024GameTheoretic}, we treat $\varphi_i$ as a \emph{best-effort Shapley approximation} satisfying the four Shapley properties (efficiency, null-player, symmetry, linearity).
Practical proxies that retain the qualitative gradient include: (a)~\emph{peak-hour energy share} $\varphi_i = E_i(t^{*})$ with $t^{*}$ the slice peak hour; (b)~\emph{leave-one-out marginals} $\varphi_i = \text{peak}(\mathcal{N}) - \text{peak}(\mathcal{N}\setminus\{i\})$; (c)~\emph{sampled-permutation} Shapley over a few hundred coalition orderings~\cite{castro2009polynomial}.
The choice of proxy is itself a design surface; \RSCI reconciles under any choice satisfying $\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r} = 1$.

\paragraph{Default mapping.}
Mapping the three families to the residual components of Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-scopes} (and its finer Scope-3 split) yields a default schema: energy-share for $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ and $\Delta^{\text{S3-FERA}}$; static-share for $\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ and the non-IT sub-components of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$; peak-attribution for $\Delta^{\text{S3-embodied-IT}}$.
This is a starting point, not a prescription: operators with finer telemetry can split residual components further or substitute other weights, and \RSCI reconciles as long as each chosen weight family sums to one across workloads in the slice.
The vision contribution of this paper is the \emph{recognition that the residual is a design surface for sustainable-behavior incentives}, not a fixed allocation rule.

\paragraph{Reconciliation by construction.}
Because $\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r} = 1$ for every component $k$, summing $\RSCI_i \cdot R_i$ across all workloads in a slice yields the top-down per-scope total:
\begin{equation}
\sum_i \RSCI_i \cdot R_i \;=\; \sum_{s,r}\Bigl(C^{\downarrow\text{S1}}_{s,r} + C^{\downarrow\text{S2}}_{s,r} + C^{\downarrow\text{S3}}_{s,r}\Bigr).
\label{eq:reconcile}
\end{equation}
Reconciliation is not an empirical property to verify; it is a structural identity that holds under \emph{any} admissible weight choice for \emph{any} residual component.

\paragraph{Properties.}
Under the default mapping, \RSCI closes the three gaps from the section opening as direct consequences of the chosen weights.
(i) \emph{Sunk-carbon}: \RSCI has no per-server $M$ term.
Embodied carbon lives in $\Delta^{\text{S3-embodied-IT}}$ and is allocated by the peak-attribution weight (Eq.~\ref{eq:w-peak}).
A workload on older, less efficient hardware draws more energy through $E_{i,s,r}\cdot I_r$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:rsci} and reports a higher \RSCI regardless of how embodied is amortised on the provider's books --- the per-request operational gradient carries the sunk-carbon-avoidance argument on its own.
(ii) \emph{Embodied incentive, and with it the peak-shaving / procurement signal}: under static-share or peak-attribution weights for the embodied component, workloads sized to fit actual demand pay less embodied than over-reserved ones --- a signal \OSCI structurally cannot supply.
Under peak-attribution weights (Eq.~\ref{eq:w-peak}) specifically, a workload running at the slice peak draws a larger embodied share than one running at the trough, \emph{even at identical energy} --- the incentive structure operators need to defer fleet expansion.
No bottom-up metric (\SCI, \OSCI, \TSCI) can supply it, because the design surface --- a residual to allocate --- does not exist without top-down anchoring.
(iii) \emph{Reconciliation}: Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}.

\paragraph{Negative residuals are admissible.}
Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-general} does not constrain the sign of $\Delta^{k}_{s,r}$.
When the bottom-up estimate $O^{k}_{s,r}$ exceeds the provider's reported $C^{\downarrow k}_{s,r}$ for a component, the residual is negative and the corresponding term in Eq.~\ref{eq:rsci} \emph{reduces} the workload's attributed footprint.
Empirically this case arises: Jacquet et al.~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy} show that current provider attribution can under-estimate operational CO$_2$ by up to 3$\times$ for high-utilisation VMs.
\RSCI absorbs such cases by construction --- reconciliation (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}) still holds --- and a negative $\Delta^{k}_{s,r}$ is itself a diagnostic: it signals that the provider's top-down report under-counts what a credible bottom-up model attributes to the slice.
The metric captures the top-down report; whether the top-down report captures physical truth is the provider's accountability, not the metric's.

\paragraph{Precision scales with data fidelity.}
\RSCI is a \emph{class} of metrics parameterised by the choice of weight family per residual component, not a fixed equation.
Its precision is bounded by three axes: bottom-up model quality (coverage and accuracy of $\varepsilon_p$), top-down reporting granularity (slice resolution + cadence), and the unit-of-work $R$.
As each improves, $\Delta$ shrinks and the residual-allocation choice matters less; reconciliation (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}) holds at any level of data fidelity and under any admissible weight choice.

\section{Closing the Gap: A Call to Action}
\label{sec:asks}

% TODO: revise after experiments. Working hypothesis: the three buckets below
% (providers / estimation / standards) are the right partition; specifics may shift
% based on which axis the experiments show is most bottle-necked.

If reconciliation-by-construction is structurally available but empirically unrealised, the question is what would change that.
We see three complementary buckets.

\paragraph{Provider asks.}
Customer carbon must be reported at \emph{billing frequency} (hourly, resource-level) to match the rate at which workloads change.
Revenue and ``equivalent revenue'' fallbacks (AWS non-foundational; \S\ref{sec:sota}) should be replaced with physical-telemetry allocation.
Customer-tool methodology must reconcile with corporate disclosure --- Microsoft's open admission that the two do not match (\S\ref{sec:sota}) is a contract violation against the customer's reporting need, not a feature.
Excluded Scope-3 categories require published materiality assessments, as the GHG Protocol already mandates.

\paragraph{Estimation techniques.}
Even at current reporting cadence, three lines of estimation can compress the residual: (a) cross-tenant pooling of $\hat\mu_{s,r}, \hat\rho_{s,r}$, since these quantities are driven by provider methodology rather than individual customers; (b) Kalman-filter state-estimation over reconciled-month sequences to track drift in PUE and hardware refresh; (c) Shapley-value attribution (or tractable proxies) for the embodied term to sharpen the peak-shaving incentive beyond plain energy-share weighting.
We do not require providers to expose proprietary telemetry --- only to report often enough that customer-side estimation can converge.

\paragraph{Standards and policy.}
The GHG Protocol is under-specified for cloud~\cite[see][]{schneider-mattia-2024}; allocation of shared infrastructure, equipment lifetimes, EAC temporal matching, and building embodied carbon are interpretive divergences arising from gaps in the standard, not violations~\cite[see ``Under-Specified GHG Protocol Areas'']{bashir2024}.
A cloud-specific gold standard must close these and require independent assurance of \emph{tool implementation}, not just the methodology document.

\paragraph{Beyond internal scheduling: user reporting and customer Scope-3.}
\S\ref{sec:rsci} frames \RSCI as an operator- and scheduler-facing signal, but reconciliation by construction (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}) enables two further roles worth flagging.
First, \RSCI is the only metric in the family that can be \emph{honestly reported to end users} of a cloud-hosted service: \OSCI shows a slice of operational electricity decoupled from the embodied, peak-driven, and overhead costs the user's request actually triggers, whereas an \RSCI number reflects all of these and therefore admits behavioral adaptation (load-shifting, request shaping, volume reduction) on grounds that match what the provider will ultimately report.
A practical concern is routing-invariance --- two identical user requests should not get different numbers because of operator placement decisions the user cannot see.
This is addressable without redefining \RSCI: because the peak-attribution weight $\varphi_i$ depends on the workload's \emph{temporal} contribution to fleet peak (when and how big), not on \emph{which physical machine} it lands on, the user-facing view can marginalise over spatial placement at fixed temporal and sizing profile.
The resulting per-(request-class, submission-time) expectation is routing-invariant for the user, preserves their temporal and sizing levers, and (by linearity of expectation, with weights still summing to one) preserves reconciliation.
Second, the same property makes \RSCI directly usable for the customer's own \emph{Scope-3 Cat-1} (purchased goods and services) accounting of cloud use: rather than relying on the provider's monthly per-account total, the customer can attribute Scope-3 across internal teams or products at workload granularity, with the guarantee that the bottom-up breakdown sums to the provider's disclosed total --- closing the loop a parallel bottom-up estimate cannot.

The residual-decomposition framework is \emph{signal-agnostic}: replace $C^{\downarrow}_{s,r}$ with a top-down water-use report and the same machinery yields a ``water per unit of work'' metric reconcilable with the provider's water disclosure.
Scaleway and OVHcloud already publish water; abiotic-resource and waste signals could follow.
A unified attribution layer would let the community extend reconciliable metrics to any sustainability dimension a provider chooses to report.

On the attribution side, the energy-share weighting in \S\ref{sec:rsci} is a deliberately simple default that is only incentive-aligned for $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$.
Kalman filtering for $\hat\mu$ state-estimation and Shapley-value attribution (or tractable proxies --- peak-hour share, leave-one-out marginals) for $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ are the two concrete refinements we see most leverage in --- the latter sharpens the peak-shaving / hardware-procurement incentive in a way energy-share weighting cannot, and crucially avoids penalising clean-grid trough usage with an embodied burden it did not cause.

estimated rather than computed exactly, reconciliation holds only in expectation.
Can we give a \emph{bounded-correlation} guarantee --- a worst-case bound on

Bashir's \TSCI and \RSCI share the goal of allocating the whole datacenter footprint to individual jobs, but tackle it from opposite directions: \TSCI aggregates bottom-up using datacenter telemetry only providers have; \RSCI inverts the problem and absorbs the same overhead from the top-down report.
Both are sound; \RSCI is the version a customer can actually compute.
A hybrid that uses \TSCI on the provider side as the top-down anchor for \RSCI on the customer side is an intriguing direction.

Multi-tenant and serverless settings reshape the residual decomposition in ways we do not address here; how to allocate the residual across tenants sharing a single warm pool is open.
Finally, standardising the bottom-up energy coefficients $\varepsilon_p$ so \RSCI is comparable across organisations is the remaining sociotechnical step.
