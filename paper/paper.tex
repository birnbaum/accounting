\begin{abstract}
There exist two largely separate approaches to quantifying the carbon footprint of cloud workloads.
Bottom-up metrics such as Software Carbon Intensity (\SCI) provide real-time signals for carbon-aware optimization, but are blind to most provider-side overheads.
Top-down reports by cloud providers can provide a comprehensive view of a tenant's carbon footprint, but are coarse, delayed, and methodologically opaque.
Because the two approaches differ in scope and reporting frequency, the current state of the art treats them as decoupled: bottom-up metrics for optimization, top-down numbers for corporate disclosure.

We argue that although the two signals can never be identical, \emph{they should be reconcilable}. 
If organizations cannot trace their bottom-up improvements through to the audited top-down report, they will lose trust in both metrics. 
In this paper, we survey the state of the art in cloud carbon accounting and propose a vision for \RSCI: a per-workload metric that re-anchors a bottom-up energy estimate to the cloud provider's top-down report through a residual bridge, which can be allocated to workloads in a way that incentivizes sustainable behavior.
We show that current provider reporting is too coarse and methodologically inconsistent reconciliation in practice, and lay out the concrete steps needed to close this gap.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A major challenge in sustainable computing is quantifying the carbon footprint of workloads. 
It is a prerequisite for determining the effectiveness of carbon-reduction strategies, for reporting to regulators and investors, and for making informed decisions about workload scheduling and adaptation. 
Two largely separate approaches exist: bottom-up estimates of operational and embodied carbon, and top-down corporate greenhouse gas (GHG) reporting. % based on provider-wide measurements and downstream allocation to tenants.

\parag{Bottom-up metrics}
Most carbon-aware computing research has focused on optimizing bottom-up metrics like operational carbon: the estimated emissions from a workload's marginal increase in energy consumption. 
This signal is straightforward to obtain (workload energy $\times$ carbon intensity) but does, for example, not capture the embodied carbon of the hardware running the workload.
To provide a more comprehensive metric, the Green Software Foundation proposed the Software Carbon Intensity (\SCI) specification~\cite{sci-spec}, codified as ISO/IEC~21031 in 2024, which expresses the carbon emitted per unit of work and folds in an embodied term.
However, Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that \SCI can produce perverse incentives for workload placement and scaling in practice. 
Furthermore, every bottom-up metric, \SCI included, can only capture what a tenant can observe or model, and is blind to most provider-side overheads.

\parag{Top-down reporting}
Due to regulatory pressure, cloud providers increasingly offer customer-facing dashboards and reports enabling tenants to estimate and disclose their cloud carbon footprint in line with the GHG Protocol~\cite{ghg-protocol-corp}.
While the reporting boundaries and allocation methods can differ significantly by provider, the general methodology is usually public and sometimes even externally audited.
However, the reported numbers are highly aggregated: 
reports mostly contain a single number per month, region, customer, and GHG Protocol scope, sometimes coarsely categorized further into service types like ``Compute'' or ``Storage''.
The reports are published about 14-21 days after the end of each month, which makes them comprehensive but unsuitable for any type of real-time optimization.

\parag{The need for reconcilability}
Today, when an engineering team improves the bottom-up metric of an internal workload, it is entirely unclear whether that improvement reaches the provider's top-down report.
The two can diverge in both directions.
The top-down report contains overheads no bottom-up model can cover without internal provider data.
And conversely, the levers a bottom-up metric does capture---such as lowering a VM's utilization and thus its power draw---may not move the reported number at all, if the provider allocates carbon to tenants by usage-hours or revenue instead of measured energy.

The Green Software Foundation explicitly endorses this separation, distinguishing ``measuring for action'' (per-workload signals that drive engineering) from ``measuring for reporting'' (organization-level inventories that satisfy regulators) and arguing that the two serve different goals and should not be conflated~\cite{gsf-measuring}.
\emph{We disagree.}
Now that regulators require firm-level GHG disclosure~\cite{eu2022csrd,SEC2024_3311275,sbti2024netzero} and carbon pricing is increasingly implemented, a per-workload metric whose gains never land in official reporting will not be funded, sustained, or trusted at scale.


\parag{Contributions}
We argue that bottom-up carbon metrics should \emph{reconcile} with the provider's top-down GHG report by construction.
Towards this, we make the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item We review the state of the art in cloud carbon accounting, including a survey of 48 providers and a matrix showing which engineering actions actually move which provider's reported number.
\item We introduce \RSCI, which anchors a bottom-up energy estimate to the provider's top-down report through a \emph{residual bridge}, so per-workload numbers sum to the reported total by construction.
\item We show that reconciliation constrains only the reported \emph{total} and leaves the per-workload allocation free, so the residual bridge can be allocated to reward sustainable behavior, such as peak-shaving and procurement-deferral, that no bottom-up-only metric can express.
\item We diagnose why \RSCI cannot yet be computed on today's provider reports, and set out concrete asks on provider reporting, customer-side estimation, and standards that would close the gap.
\end{itemize}

Under regulatory pressure~\cite{eu2022csrd,SEC2024_3311275,sbti2024netzero}, cloud providers have moved beyond annual corporate sustainability reports toward customer-facing carbon tools that attribute emissions to individual tenants, enabling Scope-3 accounting for cloud usage.
We surveyed 48 cloud service providers in May~2026 (full classification in Appendix~\ref{app:inventory}): 12 ship a first-party, per-customer dashboard, and most of these publish methodologies detailed enough to evaluate their reporting boundaries and allocation rules.

Despite differing in detail, every provider follows the same pipeline: it decides \emph{what} emissions to count, \emph{allocates} the datacenter total down to each tenant, and \emph{reports} the result at some granularity.
In practice, methodologies of providers can diverge at every stage.
Table~\ref{tab:levers} makes the divergence concrete for the nine providers whose methodologies are detailed enough to trace: it scores each against five carbon reduction \emph{levers}, i.e., actions an operator would expect to lower a workload's real-world carbon.
In particular, (i) selecting a more efficient SKU (e.g. cloud instance), (ii) right-sizing infrastructure, (iii) reducing the utilization of reserven infrastructure like a cloud instance, (iv) shifting load to a cleaner region, and (v) shifting load to a cleaner time of day.


\parag{What cloud providers cover}
Providers decompose their total datacenter emissions over the reporting period along the GHG Protocol's three scopes.
\emph{Scope~1}, direct on-site emissions such as backup-diesel combustion and refrigerant leakage, is usually negligible for most tenants today, though the increasing deployment of behind-the-meter gas generation at datacenters may change this in future.
\emph{Scope~2} is the datacenter's purchased electricity valued at the region's grid carbon intensity.\footnote{The GHG Protocol mandates dual reporting for Scope 2: \emph{location-based} figures reflect actual grid emissions, while \emph{market-based} figures net out procurement instruments like power purchase agreements (PPAs) and renewable energy certificates (RECs), which in practice often moves the market-based number to near zero. Since engineering teams have no control over the provider's procurement choices, the market-based figure is not actionable for workload optimization, which is why \SCI explicitly excludes it~\cite{sci-spec} and why we consider only the location-based figure.}
\emph{Scope~3} covers amortized cradle-to-gate hardware and, depending on the provider, building embodied carbon, upstream fuel-and-transmission losses (FERA), freight, and end-of-life.
No two of the three providers count the same set of Scope-3 categories (Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3} in the appendix), so even the headline totals are not directly comparable.

\parag{How cloud providers allocate}
Each provider then allocates the datacenter total to tenants in proportion to their share of an \emph{activity unit}, but the units are inconsistent and lean on economics to differing degrees.
AWS runs two regimes at once, physical instance-hours for services with dedicated racks, \emph{equivalent revenue} for the rest, without publishing which service falls on which side~\cite{aws-ccft-model3}.
GCP hides an economic weighting inside a physical-looking unit: it distributes each product's energy across SKUs in proportion to list price before allocating by per-SKU usage such as vCPU-hours~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}.

None of the big three allocates by \emph{measured} energy, which is certainly hard to do in virtualized infrastructure at scale.
As a result, two identical VMs of the same type and duration, one idle and one fully loaded, are attributed the same emissions (Table~\ref{tab:levers}, \emph{utilization} row).
Smaller providers show this is not fundamental: Oracle's power-based mode, Scaleway, and T~Cloud allocate compute by measured energy, so an idle and a busy instance diverge---the lever is individually feasible, just not unified in any one tool.
The activity unit also decides the \emph{SKU} lever: switching to a more efficient instance lowers the number only where the per-unit factor is grounded in energy or LCA, whereas at GCP it tracks list price, so a cheaper-but-dirtier SKU can look greener~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}.

\parag{How cloud providers report}
Customer dashboards report at the service level (for GCP, ``Compute Engine,'' ``Networking,'' \ldots), far coarser than a bottom-up metric's per-workload view: a service spans dozens of SKUs and a SKU thousands of workloads, and none of that structure survives into what is published.
A tenant sees Scope 1, 2, and 3 totals per service $\times$ region $\times$ month, released 14 to 21 days after the period closes.
This monthly cadence erases \emph{when} a tenant runs: AWS and Azure apply a monthly-average carbon intensity, and although GCP computes on hourly emission factors internally, it collapses them into a fleet-wide per-SKU-and-region average before allocating by monthly usage~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}, so shifting the same work to cleaner hours changes nothing (Table~\ref{tab:levers}, \emph{time-shifting} row).
Providers also differ on \emph{how} Scope~2 is valued: AWS, GCP, and most others expose a location-based figure that reflects the regional grid, so a cleaner region lowers the number---but Azure and T~Cloud export only the near-zero market-based figure, hiding the region lever entirely (\emph{region} row).

\section{The Issue with Bottom-Up Carbon Metrics}
\label{sec:background}

A bottom-up metric estimates a workload's carbon from quantities a tenant can measure without provider-internal data.
The de facto choice in most research papers is operational carbon, which we write as \OSCI (operational software carbon intensity) to match the terminology in Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024}:
\[
\OSCI \;=\; \frac{\E \cdot \I}{\R},
\]
the workload's energy $\E$ times its region's carbon intensity $\I$ (in gCO$_2$/kWh), per functional unit of work $\R$ (an API call, a training run, a GB stored per month).
It is straightforward, transparent, and it tracks the two levers a tenant controls directly: energy efficiency and carbon-aware timing or placement.
But it says nothing about hardware efficiency.

\[
\SCI \;=\; \frac{\E \cdot \I \;+\; \M}{\R},

However, Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that \SCI is subject to a \emph{sunk-carbon fallacy}: because \M is amortized flat over the hardware's expected lifespan, \M/\R does not respond to declining hardware efficiency. As a server ages and its energy use \E rises, the embodied component (a sunk cost) remains unchanged, so a scheduler minimizing \SCI can drift toward older, less efficient hardware, increasing total carbon emissions.
They therefore recommend sticking to \OSCI for operational decisions such as scheduling and scaling. 
We argue, however, that \OSCI is incomplete: it does not reward reserving less hardware (beyond the energy savings), purchasing lower-embodied hardware, or avoiding peak-hour demand, which ultimately determines when additional capacity must be procured.

Moreover, independent of the embodied term, \emph{any bottom-up-only metric is structurally blind to provider-side overheads} that are not explicitly modeled, and therefore cannot reconcile to the provider's top-down GHG report.
This means that improving \SCI or \OSCI does not necessarily move the audited number, and vice versa.

\paragraph{Customer-facing tools are rare.}
We surveyed 48 cloud providers in May~2026, covering hyperscalers, mid-tier and regional IaaS, CDN/edge, AI-specialised neoclouds, application-PaaS, and sustainability-positioned regional operators (full enumeration with disclosure classification in Appendix~\ref{app:inventory}).
Only twelve ship a first-party, per-customer carbon dashboard --- the bar for a customer to source a GHG-Protocol-compliant Scope-3 Cat-1 input from their cloud spend.\footnote{We exclude marketplace third-party SaaS such as Huella Cloud on Huawei Cloud (vendor: SGS Solutions), since the per-provider allocation is not auditable against the provider's own emission factors. Exoscale's calculator is borderline (Exoscale-branded surface, CloudAssess engine by Resilio/Kleis) and is omitted.}
Of the remaining 36, fourteen publish corporate-level Scope~1/2/3 disclosures without any per-customer surface; the other 22 publish only marketing-grade renewable/PUE claims or nothing at all.
Most starkly, the fastest-growing segment --- AI/GPU clouds (CoreWeave, Lambda, Crusoe, Nebius, RunPod, Together, Replicate, Hyperstack, Verda, Fireworks~AI, Paperspace) --- has \emph{zero} per-customer tools.
Compute capacity for AI training and inference is being built at the fastest pace in cloud history with the \emph{least} customer carbon transparency.
The rest of this section focuses on the twelve per-customer tools.

\paragraph{Four axes of variation.}
Every customer tool computes the same shape:
\[
\mathrm{customer\,carbon} \;=\;
\textstyle\sum_{r}\, u_{r}\,\cdot\,f_{r} \;+\; \mathrm{overheads},
\]
where $u_{r}$ is an activity unit (vCPU-hours, instance-hours, bytes, CPU-time, GB-month, \$-spend, server-hours) and $f_{r}$ is a per-unit factor.
Provider variation lives on four orthogonal axes: (A1)~which activity unit, (A2)~how $f_{r}$ is derived, (A3)~embodied carbon treatment, (A4)~idle/overhead allocation.
Eleven of the twelve customer tools differ on the two most discriminating axes (A1, A2); T Cloud Public is the twelfth (launched 2026-02-23) but has not disclosed its per-resource allocation rule.
The prior literature's three-family taxonomy (usage-time / traffic / component-LCA) puts AWS-foundational and OVHcloud-dedicated in different families even though both reduce to $\sum u_{r} f_{r}$, and we replace it with the per-axis decomposition below.


\paragraph{Measured energy.}
How well _that unit tracks the customer's actual energy draw.
For network, bytes are approximately linear in energy, so byte-share allocation is structurally sound (Cloudflare, Fastly-Delivery, Akamai's byte component).
For compute the relationship is highly non-linear (an idle server still draws on the order of half its full-load power) so vCPU-hours, instance-hours, and \$-spend systematically misweight idle and busy workloads.
Four customer-surface allocations approach energy-direct, in decreasing concreteness: \emph{Scaleway} combines per-component LCA with actual measured CPU consumption and published Boavizta consumption profiles for the monthly report, so an idle and a fully-loaded VM at the same SKU receive different allocations;\footnote{\url{https://www.scaleway.com/en/docs/environmental-footprint/}; per-product calculation pages compiled in our reference set.}
\emph{Akamai} meters kWh per server and per network device in its colocations\footnote{Akamai 2025 emissions-reporting policy and customer-reporting blog series.} and allocates DC-total energy by ``byte utilization and machine utilization'';
\emph{Oracle}'s power-based mode tracks kWh by service workload (LBM~+~MBM) but does not disclose the shared-hardware allocation algorithm;\footnote{\url{https://docs.oracle.com/en-us/iaas/releasenotes/governance/carbon-analysis-powercalculation.htm}, June~2025.}
and \emph{GCP-internal} measures power per machine via Borg telemetry but deliberately re-projects to vCPU-h~$\times$~list-price at the customer surface~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}.
The remaining compute-allocation tools (AWS, Azure, IBM, Alibaba, OVHcloud-currently) propagate a usage-unit proxy: an idle and a fully-loaded VM of the same SKU and duration produce the same number.
T Cloud Public claims a utilization-share allocation from measured device-level power but has not yet published its per-resource formula, so the customer-side weighting cannot be independently checked.

\paragraph{Coverage and verification are shallower than the dashboards suggest.}
Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3} breaks the Big-3 Scope-3 perimeter down by GHG-Protocol category.
No two providers cover the same set: AWS includes FERA and full Cat~2 capital goods (with buildings); GCP adds business travel and employee commuting but excludes end-of-life; Azure is the only Big-3 to include Cat~12 end-of-life yet excludes both FERA and building embodied carbon.
None publishes the materiality assessment the GHG Protocol requires to justify a category exclusion.
Headline numbers are therefore not directly comparable.
Third-party assurance is similarly shallow: AWS's Apex statement covers Scope-3 Cat~2/3/4 \emph{methodology} only, GCP's Fraunhofer review covers the LCA methodology only, Azure's WSP statement covers the Scope-3 \emph{document} only; \textbf{no Big-3 customer tool has been audited end-to-end}, and only Alibaba's Energy~Expert carries third-party \emph{data} assurance (Bureau Veritas, ISO~14064-3) of the per-customer numbers.
The temporal surface is similarly thin: \textbf{only Scaleway and Fastly publish daily customer-facing data}; the other nine, including all of the Big-3, are monthly; \emph{no} customer tool publishes hourly customer-allocated emissions, so any carbon-aware-scheduling claim today rests on signals outside the customer tool itself.

\paragraph{The internal/external gap is structural.}
Microsoft's 2021 Scope-3 transparency whitepaper, attached to its own description of the customer-facing tool, states that ``\emph{the underlying methodologies and emissions findings generated from the calculator will differ from those reflected in Microsoft's corporate disclosure}''~\cite[p.~2, fn.~2]{microsoft-s3-2021}, and the January~2026 Azure methodology page repeats the point in operational terms: ``\emph{usage for emissions calculations might not match your Microsoft usage for billing purposes}''~\cite{azure-methodology-2026}.
GCP measures machine-level power via Borg hourly but re-projects to a monthly \emph{list-price-proportional} SKU factor at the customer surface, and Schneider \& Mattia note (\S3.6) that ``\emph{customers do not receive a signal about how switching from SKU~A to SKU~B can reduce their actual emissions, but rather their allocated emissions based on list prices}''~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}.
AWS's Model~3.0 defines a foundational/non-foundational service split but publishes neither the service list nor the share of emissions allocated by equivalent revenue~\cite{aws-ccft-model3}.
The pattern is not three isolated quirks: every customer tool ships a methodologically distinct, thinner subset of what the provider measures internally, and the size of that gap is the residual that \S\ref{sec:rsci} formalises through \RSCI.
Bottom-up engines such as BoaviztAPI~\cite{simon2024boaviztapi} and Cinergy~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy} reach the same verdict from the opposite side and are the natural feed for the bottom-up term in \RSCI; they do not themselves reconcile to the provider report.

\section{Reconciling Bottom-Up and Top-Down}
\label{sec:rsci}

We introduce \emph{Reconcilable \SCI} (\RSCI), a family of per-workload metrics that anchors a bottom-up energy estimate to the provider's top-down report and allocates the residual across workloads by a weight matched to its physical driver.
The \RSCI numbers sums to the reported totals by construction, which makes improvements traceable.
%This solves two problems of current bottom-up metrics.
%First, per-workload numbers sum to the reported total by construction, so a bottom-up improvement is traceable to the audited number.
%Second, an embodied term is either perverse (\SCI's sunk-carbon fallacy~\cite{bashir2024}) or absent (\OSCI), and routing embodied through the residual escapes both.
% Because reconciliation pins only the slice total and not the allocation, the weights are free to reward peak-shaving, procurement deferral, and load-shifting that no bottom-up-only metric expresses.

\parag{Setup}
Let $i$ index a \emph{workload}: a distinct billable unit a tenant runs, like a service or a batch job.
A reporting \emph{slice} is the provider's finest published granularity (service $\times$ region $\times$ month).
For example, writing \OSCI using this per-workload notation, we get:
\begin{equation}
\OSCI_i \;=\; \frac{\E_{i,s}\cdot\I_s}{\R_i},
\label{eq:osci-i}
\end{equation}
where $\E_{i,s}$ is the workload's energy within the slice, $\I_s$ the slice's location-based carbon intensity, and $\R_i$ the functional unit totalled over the period (requests, tokens, GB).
%Time enters only \emph{within} $\E_{i,s}\cdot\I_s$, which abbreviates the hourly sum $\sum_t \E_i(t)\,\I_{s,t}$; the residual is anchored at the provider's coarser cadence.
%\OSCI is all a tenant can observe without provider-internal data, and the only bottom-up input \RSCI uses.

% The provider publishes only one such figure per slice, but \RSCI refines it into per-workload values.
% While the operational term can be evaluated at any finer cadence, whereas the residual is known only per period.

\OSCI, \SCI and \RSCI all build on a bottom-up energy estimate.
We can, for example, derive $\E_{i,s}$ from per-resource quantities,
\begin{equation}
\E_{i,s} \;=\; \mathrm{PUE}\cdot\sum_{p} q_p \cdot \varepsilon_p,
\label{eq:bu-energy}
\end{equation}
where $p$ indexes resource types (vCPU-hours, GPU-hours, GB-hours of storage, I/O ops, network GB), $\varepsilon_p$ is a per-type energy coefficient from open-data engines such as BoaviztAPI~\cite{simon2024boaviztapi} or Cinergy~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy}.
$\mathrm{PUE}$ (power usage effectiveness) is an optional factor for modeling facility overhead.

\parag{Reconciliation}
A bottom-up model cannot capture all energy usage within Scope 2: coefficients are approximate, idle energy cannot be modeled, and a fixed PUE ignores that real facility overhead varies with season and weather.
Emissions in Scope 1 and 3 are even less observable.
For each slice, we define the \emph{residual} emissions as the difference between the provider's top-down reported total carbon footprint $C^{\downarrow}_{s}$ and the bottom-up estimate:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{s} \;=\; C^{\downarrow}_{s} \;-\; \sum_i \E_{i,s}\,\I_s .
\label{eq:residual-total}
\end{equation}
\RSCI distributes this residual across the slice's workloads by weights.
It uses the same operational term as \OSCI, but adds a residual term, taken over one or more components $\Delta^{c}_s$ (with $\sum_c \Delta^{c}_s = \Delta_s$), each distributed across the slice's workloads by a weight $w^{c}_{i,s}$ that sums to one over them ($\sum_{i \in s} w^{c}_{i,s} = 1$):
\begin{equation}
\RSCI_i \;=\; \frac{\overbrace{\E_{i,s}\cdot\I_s}^\text{operational term} \;+\; \overbrace{\textstyle\sum_c w^{c}_{i,s}\,\Delta^{c}_{s}}^\text{residual term}}{\R_i}.
\label{eq:rsci}
\end{equation}
Attributed carbon then reconciles by construction: scaling each score back to carbon by its $\R_i$, the slice's \RSCI values sum to the reported total,
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i \in s} \RSCI_i\,\R_i \;=\; \sum_i \E_{i,s}\,\I_s \;+\; \Delta_s \;=\; \textstyle\sum_k C^{\downarrow k}_{s}.
\label{eq:reconcile}
\end{equation}
A workload spanning several slices (e.g., multiple services or regions) sums its per-slice attributions, and since each balances on its own, reconciliation composes to the fleet total.

This identity holds for \emph{any} partition of the residual into components and \emph{any} normalized weights.
Both are free, and different choices incentivise different behavior.


\parag{Decomposing the residual}
The simplest allocation choice would be to split the full residual $\Delta_s$ evenly, giving each of the slice's $N_s$ workloads a share $w_i = 1/N_s$.
This reconciles, but creates no incentives.
To improve this, we first decompose the residual the way the provider reports it, by GHG scope.
Let $C_{s}^{\downarrow\text{S1}}$, $C_{s}^{\downarrow\text{S2}}$, $C_{s}^{\downarrow\text{S3}}$ denote the top-down reported per-slice, per-scope emissions.
Since our bottom-up model estimates only operational Scope~2 carbon, the per-scope residuals (summing to $\Delta_s$) are
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\text{S1}}_{s} = C^{\downarrow\text{S1}}_{s}, \qquad
\Delta^{\text{S2}}_{s} = C^{\downarrow\text{S2}}_{s} - \sum_i \E_{i,s}\,\I_s, \qquad
\Delta^{\text{S3}}_{s} = C^{\downarrow\text{S3}}_{s},
\label{eq:residual-scopes}
\end{equation}
$\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ absorbs diesel and refrigerants;
$\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ idle capacity and energy-model error;
$\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ embodied hardware and the rest of Scope~3.

The scope split mirrors the provider's reporting but not the levers a tenant controls. 
What matters for incentives is a second axis, whether an emission scales with energy usage or with provisioned capacity.
We therefore propose regrouping the three scope residuals into an energy-driven $\Delta^{\text{en}}_{s}$ and a capacity-driven $\Delta^{\text{cap}}_{s}$ term, writing $\beta^{k}\in[0,1]$ for the energy-driven fraction of scope $k$'s residual:
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\text{en}}_{s} = \sum_{k} \beta^{k}\,\Delta^{k}_{s}, \qquad
\Delta^{\text{cap}}_{s} = \sum_{k} (1-\beta^{k})\,\Delta^{k}_{s}.
\label{eq:residual-levers}
\end{equation}
Scope~1 carries no energy-driven part ($\beta^{\text{S1}}=0$).
Scope~2 mixes energy-driven terms (unmodeled overhead, energy-model error) with capacity-driven ones (idle power), so $\beta^{\text{S2}}$ depends on how well the bottom-up model captures the energy-driven part.
Scope~3 is mostly capacity-driven (embodied hardware and buildings), with a smaller energy-driven part (electricity upstream, i.e.\ FERA).

Since $\Delta^{\text{en}}_s + \Delta^{\text{cap}}_s = \Delta_s$, any $\beta$ preserves reconciliation. 
It only tunes the incentive structure, so rough estimates suffice.

\parag{Allocating the residual}
The energy-driven residual $\Delta^{\text{en}}$ scales with electricity drawn, so we split it by energy share,
\begin{equation}
w^{\text{en}}_{i,s} = \frac{\E_{i,s}}{\E_s} , \qquad \E_s = \sum_i \E_{i,s}.
\label{eq:w-energy}
\end{equation}

The capacity-driven residual $\Delta^{\text{cap}}$ is not a direct cost of running a workload, yet it must still be allocated.
Sharma and Fuerst split such shared costs evenly across workloads~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless}.
We instead attribute by peak capacity, following Shapley-based accounting for shared datacentre power~\cite{islam2016energyaccounting} and the peak-provisioning argument of Han et al.~\cite{HotCarbon2024GameTheoretic}: the workloads running during peak times drive the next server purchase.
The residual then takes the weight
\begin{equation}
w^{\text{cap}}_{i,s} = \frac{\varphi_i}{\sum_j \varphi_j} ,
\label{eq:w-peak}
\end{equation}

where $\varphi_i$ is workload $i$'s Shapley share~\cite{shapley1953value} of the period's peak capacity. 
Computing exact Shapley is expensive, but proxies (peak-hour energy share or sampled permutations~\cite{castro2009polynomial}) can approximate the ordering and incentive well.
As $\varphi_i$ depends on the whole period's load profile, the weight is inherently retrospective and can only be computed once the period closes.
Section~\ref{sec:asks} discusses estimating it ahead of the report to provide a real-time metric.

\section{Example}
\label{sec:demo}

To illustrate \RSCI's reconciliation and incentives, we work one example end to end: a single workload on a single node, reporting carbon per token served.

\parag{Scenario}
An organization reserves a bare-metal 8$\times$H100 node to serve an internal, employee-facing assistant, a copilot over internal documents, over a three-day window.
The node draws about $2.5$\,kW idle and up to $10.2$\,kW at full load~\cite{nvidia-dgx-h100-datasheet}, so its power tracks demand, which we drive from a real Azure inference trace~\cite{stojkovic-dynamollm-2025} priced against a real CAISO grid-intensity trace~\cite{electricitymaps2026}.
After the fact, the provider reports the node's footprint top-down: about $80$\,kg Scope~2 and $20$\,kg Scope~3, $100$\,kg in total (we omit Scope~1).
We ask how close each per-workload metric comes to that $100$\,kg, and what behaviour it rewards along the way (Figure~\ref{fig:demo}); magnitudes are illustrative, so we claim only direction and ordering.

\centering

\parag{\OSCI}
The operational metric counts only the marginal energy the work draws above idle, scaled by the datacentre PUE ($1.2$) and the hourly grid intensity.
It rewards the levers a tenant controls day to day: using less energy, and shifting flexible work into cleaner hours.
By construction it stops there, so the node's idle baseline and its embodied hardware stay invisible to it.
Here it reaches about $48$\,kg, under half the reported total.
Its one advantage over \SCI is that, lacking any embodied term, it cannot be gamed by depreciated hardware, the sunk-carbon fallacy~\cite{bashir2024}.

\parag{\SCI}
\SCI folds in an embodied term $M$: the node's cradle-to-gate carbon amortised over its lifespan and divided across the tokens it serves in that time~\cite{sci-spec}.\footnote{We take the node's cradle-to-gate embodied carbon as ${\approx}2500$\,kg: NVIDIA reports $1312$\,kg for the 8-GPU HGX~H100 baseboard~\cite{nvidia2025hgxh100pcf}, plus CPUs, memory, storage, NICs, power supplies, and cooling. The \SCI spec sets $M=\mathit{TE}\cdot(\mathit{TiR}/\mathit{EL})\cdot(\mathit{RR}/\mathit{ToR})$; for this single-tenant node ($\mathit{RR}/\mathit{ToR}{=}1$) it is that embodied carbon prorated over a 4-year lifespan. The per-token term stays well below the operational one even at twice the embodied carbon or half the lifespan.}
The intent is to price hardware, but per token the amortised figure is small, about a tenth of the operational term, so the total barely moves and still falls well short of the report.
Worse, the per-server $M$ reintroduces the fallacy \OSCI avoided: a workload on older, written-down hardware is charged \emph{less} embodied carbon, rewarding the tenant for keeping inefficient machines in service~\cite{bashir2024}.
\SCI is thus both insufficient and, on embodied, perverse.

\parag{\RSCI}
\RSCI closes the gap by construction.
It keeps \OSCI's operational term and adds the \emph{residual}: the difference between the reported total and the bottom-up estimate, here about $52$\,kg.
Following \S\ref{sec:rsci} we split it by physical driver and allocate each part by a matched weight: an energy-driven part (about $13$\,kg, upstream electricity and model error), shared by energy drawn and flat per token; and a capacity-driven part (about $39$\,kg, idle and embodied), shared by peak-attribution and rising with load.
The energy band rewards efficiency like \OSCI.
The capacity band makes a token served in a busy hour, or one held on over-reserved capacity, pay a larger share of the fixed cost of provisioning, the incentive \OSCI drops and \SCI mishandles.
Crucially, it prices this capacity with no per-server embodied model, so it carries none of \SCI's sunk-carbon fallacy: aging hardware simply draws more energy and reports a higher \RSCI.
And because reconciliation fixes only the total, the weights stay free to reward peak-shaving and procurement deferral while still summing to the reported $100$\,kg; when no residual can be recovered they vanish and \RSCI degrades gracefully to \OSCI.
In this trace the busier hours also tend to be somewhat dirtier, so a tenant that could shift deferrable work off-peak would lower \RSCI twice over: a cleaner operational term and a smaller capacity share.

One caveat: \RSCI is retrospective, since the residual is known only once the provider publishes its report, so it cannot be computed in real time.
This does not blunt its incentives: because the weights are fixed in advance, the effect of an action is known at decision time even though the value settles later, just as a peak demand charge shapes consumption long before the bill arrives.
Across the five properties in Table~\ref{tab:properties}, only \RSCI satisfies all: it rewards every operational lever, prices capacity and embodied carbon, avoids the sunk-carbon fallacy, can reward procurement deferral, and reconciles to the provider's report.

\section{Barriers to Practical Adoption}
\label{sec:practice}

% real-time: covered in §4 Properties (retrospective by design) and §7 (estimator sketch)

\S\ref{sec:demo} showed that once the residual is known, \RSCI reconciles by construction, and Table~\ref{tab:levers} claims it responds to every lever.
Both rest on a condition the example granted for free: that the provider's report supplies a usable residual.
The reconciliation identity (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}) holds for any residual and any normalized weights, so \RSCI is always computable in principle.
Whether it is \emph{useful} depends on three properties of the reported number.
It must be \emph{present}, so that Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-total} has an anchor to difference against.
It must be \emph{physically grounded}, so that each residual component has a driver a weight can attach to; otherwise the score reconciles but does not move when a workload changes its behavior.
And it must be \emph{trustworthy}, stable and auditable enough to account against.
We probed three real customer exports.
Each satisfies \RSCI's arithmetic and fails a different one of these properties.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{What recovery requires.} State the criterion as one requirement per weight family (\S\ref{sec:rsci}): the residual must be \emph{decomposed} into components; it needs a \emph{physical} (energy-based) allocation basis, or the energy-share weight cannot be formed; and it needs \emph{resource-level, billing-frequency} granularity, or the peak and temporal weights have nothing to attach to. Today's report supplies none of the three cleanly.
\item \textbf{Evidence: real exports, each defeating one requirement.}
  \begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{AWS Frankfurt} --- allocation basis: most of the reported total sits in the revenue-allocated ``Other'' bucket, an undifferentiated lump with no physical driver to weight against. \todo{re-derive the $\sim$70\,\% figure in the consistent pass.}
    \item \emph{Azure Frankfurt} --- the anchor itself: exported location-based Scope-2 is near zero, so the quantity the operational term reconciles against is missing and the region and time levers leave no recoverable response. \todo{re-derive.}
    \item \emph{Scaleway} --- partial existence proof: daily granularity \emph{exists} (the levers are individually feasible), but the daily number is flat under load and idle\,$\approx$\,stress is confounded, so the \emph{union} \RSCI needs is unrealised. \todo{data pending; keep qualitative until the re-run lands.}
  \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Diagnostic read-out.} Line the failures up against the not-reflected cells of Table~\ref{tab:levers} (utilization, time-shifting, peak): \RSCI could carry a faithful response to each, but the report exposes nothing of the matching shape, so the residual --- not the workload --- absorbs the lever. The residual is not merely large but \emph{undifferentiated}, and its size measures how much of each lever's real-carbon effect the provider declines to expose. Time-shifting is the concrete instance: a workload moved to a cleaner hour lowers its operational term, but the provider's monthly-average Scope-2 does not move, so the reduction is not lost but \emph{relocated} into a growing $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ --- a measured quantity of how much clean-hour behavior the coarse methodology declines to credit (reconciliation forbids manufacturing a fleet-level reduction the audited books do not show). This is \RSCI as a diagnostic.
\item \textbf{Close (bridge to \S\ref{sec:asks}).} The barrier is structural (coarse, delayed, opaque) but not fundamental: Scaleway and Oracle's power-based mode show the pieces are individually feasible, so it is a reporting \emph{choice}, which \S\ref{sec:asks} takes up.
\end{itemize}

Reconciliation by construction is structurally available, yet \S\ref{sec:practice} shows it is empirically unrealised on today's reports.
Closing that gap is a shared task for providers and standards bodies, and it opens further roles for the metric.

\paragraph{What providers must report.}
Customer carbon must be reported at \emph{billing frequency} and resource granularity, so that the temporal and utilization levers of Table~\ref{tab:levers} land in the number rather than the residual.
Providers already hold the data: GCP computes its location-based figure on hourly emission factors before collapsing them to a monthly average (\S\ref{sec:sota}), so finer reporting is a disclosure choice, not a measurement problem.
Revenue and ``equivalent-revenue'' fallbacks (AWS non-foundational; \S\ref{sec:sota}) should give way to physical-telemetry allocation, and each customer tool should reconcile with the provider's corporate disclosure: Microsoft's open admission that the two do not match (\S\ref{sec:sota}) is a defect against the customer's reporting need.
Excluded Scope-3 categories require the published materiality assessments the GHG Protocol already mandates.
We do not ask providers to expose proprietary telemetry, only to report often enough that customer-side estimation, such as cross-tenant pooling of methodology-driven overheads and state-estimation over reconciled-month sequences, can converge on the residual.

\paragraph{What standards must fix.}
The GHG Protocol is under-specified for cloud~\cite[see][]{schneider-mattia-2024}: shared-infrastructure allocation, equipment lifetimes, temporal matching of energy attributes, and building embodied carbon are interpretive gaps in the standard, not violations~\cite[see ``Under-Specified GHG Protocol Areas'']{bashir2024}.
A cloud-specific gold standard should close these and require independent assurance of the \emph{tool implementation}, not just the methodology document.

\paragraph{Outlook.}
Two roles beyond operator scheduling follow directly from reconciliation (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}).
\RSCI is the only metric in its family that can be \emph{honestly reported to end users}, since it reflects the embodied, peak, and overhead costs a request actually triggers rather than operational electricity alone; a per-(request-class, submission-time) view marginalised over spatial placement keeps this routing-invariant while preserving the user's temporal and sizing levers.
The same decomposition supports a customer's own \emph{Scope-3 Cat-1} accounting of cloud use, attributing the provider's disclosed total across internal teams at workload granularity with the guarantee that the parts sum to the whole.
It extends past carbon, too: swap the carbon report for a provider's water disclosure and the construction yields a reconcilable ``water per unit of work'' metric, with Bashir's \TSCI available as the provider-side anchor for \RSCI on the customer side.

\paragraph{Toward a real-time signal.}
As defined, \RSCI is a \emph{retrospective} metric: the residuals $\Delta_s$ and the weight denominators ($E_s$, the slice peak) are known only once the provider's report lands, weeks to months late.
Its native use is therefore \emph{accounting} --- faithful allocation of an audited total --- and its incentives bite at reporting cadence, like a carbon chargeback, rather than in a closed scheduling loop.
Turning it into a real-time signal is an open problem: predict each residual component ahead of the report.
This is plausible precisely because a good bottom-up model leaves a residual dominated by \emph{slow-moving, methodology-driven} overheads (PUE, embodied intensity, allocation constants) rather than workload dynamics, so it should be learnable from the sequence of reconciled months.
No single per-Wh multiplier captures this under the per-component schema --- different components ride different drivers (electricity, fleet existence, peak share) and need separate estimators --- but each is a low-dimensional, slowly-varying quantity well suited to online state estimation, cross-tenant pooling of shared overheads, and Kalman-filter-style updating (Sharma and Fuerst apply exactly this class of filter to online \emph{marginal-energy} profiling of serverless functions~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless}; here it would be lifted from energy to the residual).
Absent any history the estimators default to zero residual and \RSCI degrades gracefully to \OSCI, the prior-art operational metric.

\paragraph{Further open problems.}
Two narrower questions remain: allocating the residual across tenants sharing serverless warm pools; and bounding reconciliation error once the peak-attribution weight $\varphi_i$ is estimated rather than computed exactly, which would upgrade the guarantee from ``reconcilable by construction'' to provably $\varepsilon$-close under practical attribution.

\item \textit{Sustainability-positioned regionals}: regional IaaS or DC operators whose primary differentiator is environmental, e.g. Nordic siting, heat reuse, hydro-powered grids.

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item \statusA{Per-customer tool}: first-party dashboard or API exposing workload-level emissions per tenant.
\item \statusB{Assured GHG report}: Scope~1/2/3 corporate inventory with external assurance (SBTi-validated, CSRD-baselined, ISO~14064-certified, or otherwise third-party-audited). The corporate number is reliable enough to inherit downstream as Scope~3.
\item \statusB{Self-reported}: published Scope~1/2/3 inventory, \emph{or} substantive quantitative operational disclosure (per-DC carbon intensity, PUE, energy mix, EMAS environmental statement), without external GHG assurance.
\item \statusC{Marketing claim}: forward net-zero pledge, ``100\,\% renewable'' / REC-backed statement, or qualitative sustainability strategy, but no quantified disclosure.
\item \statusC{No disclosure}: nothing surfaced through our search.
\end{itemize}
