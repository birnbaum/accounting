\section{Background: SCI and the Sunk Carbon Fallacy}
\label{sec:background}

The community's per-workload carbon metric is the \emph{Software Carbon Intensity} (\textsc{sci}),
codified by the Green Software Foundation and adopted as ISO/IEC 21031~\cite{sci-spec}:
\[
\textsc{sci} \;=\; \frac{E \cdot I \;+\; M}{R},
\]
where $E$ is the energy consumed running the workload, $I$ the carbon intensity of that
energy, $M$ the amortised embodied carbon of the server, and $R$ a unit of work
(API call, transaction, training run, $\ldots$). The metric is real-time-sensitive,
applicable from a single function to a datacenter, and intended to drive engineering
optimisation.

Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that \textsc{sci} contains a structural bug they
call the \emph{sunk-carbon fallacy}: because $M$ is amortised at a flat rate over the
device's expected lifespan, the per-unit-work $M$ does not respond to declining
efficiency, so as hardware ages, $E$ grows but $M$/$R$ does not, and a carbon-aware
scheduler optimising \textsc{sci} is biased toward older, less efficient
servers --- the opposite of what total-footprint minimisation requires. Provider
amortisation conventions (AWS/Azure 6\,yr, GCP 4\,yr; all zero out past EL ---
Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3}) sharpen this into the limit case where past-EL hardware
contributes $M=0$ per request; we note that the SCI specification itself is silent on
$\text{TiR}>\text{EL}$, so the $M=0$ reading is a provider operationalisation rather
than an SCI mandate, but it is the reading customers see today. To fix the underlying
fallacy, Bashir et al.\ introduce two refinements:
\[
\textsc{o-sci} = \frac{E \cdot I}{R}, \qquad
\textsc{t-sci} = \frac{tO + tM}{R},
\]
where \textsc{o-sci} drops the embodied term entirely, and \textsc{t-sci} keeps it but
allocates the \emph{entire datacenter's} operational and embodied carbon (active and
idle servers alike) across jobs by time, idle, and usage fractions. Both avoid the
sunk-carbon fallacy --- the per-job number no longer depends on the specific server it
lands on. \textsc{o-sci} is cheap to compute; \textsc{t-sci} is sound but demands
datacenter-wide telemetry that public-cloud customers cannot
access~\cite[\S3.1]{bashir2024}.

\textsc{o-sci} resolves the sunk-carbon fallacy by dropping embodied carbon entirely,
but this is not free. Without an embodied term, \textsc{o-sci} (i) does not reward
frugal hardware reservation beyond what energy savings already imply; (ii) gives no
signal to prefer hardware procured with low-embodied processes; and (iii) does not
disincentivize peak-hour use --- the lever that delays new hardware purchases in
capacity-constrained datacenters. \textsc{t-sci} would restore these incentives by
construction, but, as Bashir notes, it requires comprehensive datacenter-level
information that public-cloud customers cannot
access~\cite[\S3.1]{bashir2024}.\footnote{The tSCI ambition --- allocating the full
datacenter footprint to individual jobs --- is closely related to ours, but tackled
from the opposite direction. We return to this parallel in \S\ref{sec:future-work}.}

There is a second, structurally distinct weakness shared by every variant in the
\textsc{sci} family: none of them connects to the cloud provider's customer-allocated
GHG report. An engineering team that improves \textsc{sci}, \textsc{o-sci}, or
\textsc{t-sci} cannot point to a matching change in the company's corporate
sustainability disclosure. A metric whose improvement does not land in audited
reporting will not be funded, sustained, or trusted at scale --- and the cloud
provider's number does not move in response to bottom-up optimisation. The next
section grounds this second critique in what providers actually publish today.

\paragraph{Three non-summable methodology families.}
The Tier-A tools differ in what they take as the \emph{unit of allocation}, and the
three resulting families are not directly comparable.
\emph{Usage-time allocated} (AWS, GCP, Azure, Oracle, IBM, Alibaba) treats the
\emph{rental-line} as the proxy: carbon is proportional to vCPU-hours or instance-hours
of an SKU, possibly re-weighted by list price (GCP), revenue (AWS non-foundational),
or a ``normalized cost'' metric (Azure). Embodied carbon is folded into the per-SKU
factor and amortised flat.
\emph{Traffic-allocated} (Akamai, Cloudflare, Fastly) takes the \emph{work-volume} as
the proxy: bytes delivered or CPU-time consumed; embodied hardware is largely or
entirely excluded.
\emph{Component-LCA per resource} (OVHcloud, Scaleway) builds the number from a
\emph{physical inventory}: every reserved component (rack, cores, GB~RAM, TB~SSD)
contributes a published LCA factor, plus an operational energy term.
The three families allocate different slices of provider emissions in different units;
adding or directly benchmarking numbers across them is not meaningful without an
explicit residual bridge --- precisely the gap \S\ref{sec:rsci} formalises.

\paragraph{Two structural failure modes in the Big-3.}
Two findings from Tables~\ref{tab:tier-a} and~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3} land hardest for
the reconcilability argument that follows in \S\ref{sec:rsci}. First, Azure's customer
dashboard does not cover Azure~Government, any China region, or the German Sovereign
regions --- coverage is not uniform across the provider's own footprint. Second, the
January~2026 Azure emissions-methodology page states verbatim that
``\emph{usage for emissions calculations might not match your Microsoft usage for
billing purposes}'' --- a provider-acknowledged divergence between the billing surface
and the emissions surface. Together, these are not isolated bugs but instances of a
broader pattern: the customer-facing surface is a thinner, methodologically distinct
subset of what the provider measures internally.

\paragraph{The internal/external gap is structural --- and is the rSCI residual.}
Concrete evidence from three of the Big-3:
(i) Microsoft's 2021 Scope-3 Whitepaper (footnote~2) acknowledges that the customer-tool
methodology and the corporate-disclosure methodology have not been
reconciled~\cite{microsoft-s3-2021};\footnote{\textbf{TODO --- verification pending.} The PDF currently in \texttt{references/carbon\_accounting\_methodologies/azure/} is the 2018/2020 Microsoft+WSP cloud-carbon study, not the 2021 Scope-3 Whitepaper. Verify the footnote-2 wording against the actual 2021 doc before camera-ready.}
(ii) GCP measures machine-level power via Borg telemetry hourly, but the customer
surface is monthly and uses a \emph{list-price-proportional} SKU factor that does not
reflect the underlying physical
measurement~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024};
(iii) AWS's Model~3.0.1 (Apr~2026) defines a foundational/non-foundational service split but
publishes neither the service list nor the emissions share allocated by equivalent
revenue~\cite{aws-ccft-model3}. What providers measure internally is methodologically
richer than what they ship to customers; the size of that gap is precisely the
residual that \S\ref{sec:rsci} makes explicit through the rSCI decomposition.

\section{rSCI: Reconciling Bottom-Up and Top-Down}
\label{sec:rsci}

% NOTE: framing wrapper depends on §5 experiments. Math is committed; opening list
% of "four gaps closed" follows the §2 critique structure.

We introduce \emph{Reconcilable SCI} (rSCI), a per-workload metric designed to close
four gaps identified in \S\ref{sec:background} and \S\ref{sec:sota}:
(i) the sunk-carbon fallacy of \textsc{sci};
(ii) the loss of embodied-carbon incentives in \textsc{o-sci};
(iii) the absence of a peak-shaving / hardware-procurement signal in \textsc{o-sci};
(iv) the disconnect between every \textsc{sci} variant and the cloud provider's
top-down carbon report.
rSCI does so by anchoring a bottom-up energy model to the provider's top-down per-scope
report via per-slice residual bridges.

\paragraph{Setup.}
Let $i$ index \emph{workload instances} --- one evaluation of a workload over period
$t_i$. The provider issues a top-down report broken into Scope-1, Scope-2, and
Scope-3 totals at the level of a \emph{reporting slice} indexed by service $s$ and
region $r$ (the finest granularity providers publish: per-month, per-project, per-service).
Let $C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S1}}$, $C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S2}}$, and
$C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S3}}$ denote the reported per-scope emissions of slice $(s,r)$.

\paragraph{Bottom-up energy.}
For workload $i$ in slice $(s,r)$ we estimate energy from per-resource quantities:
\[
E_{i,s,r} \;=\; \sum_{p} q_p \cdot \varepsilon_p \quad [\text{Wh}],
\]
where $p$ indexes resource types (vCPU-hours, GPU-hours, GB-hours of storage, I/O ops,
network GB) and $\varepsilon_p$ is a per-type energy coefficient. The implied bottom-up
operational carbon for the slice is
$O_{s,r} = \sum_i E_{i,s,r}\cdot I_r$, with $I_r$ the location-based grid intensity
of region $r$. Unobservable resource types simply enlarge the residual below.

\paragraph{Residual bridges.}
We define one residual per scope:
\[
\Delta_{s,r}^{\text{S1}} = C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S1}},
\quad
\Delta_{s,r}^{\text{S2}} = C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S2}} - O_{s,r},
\quad
\Delta_{s,r}^{\text{S3}} = C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S3}}.
\]
$\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ absorbs diesel and refrigerant emissions (bottom-up un-modellable).
$\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ absorbs PUE, idle capacity, market-vs-location adjustments, and
energy-model error. $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ absorbs embodied hardware, building embodied,
FERA, upstream transport, and the other Scope-3 categories enumerated in
Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3}.

\paragraph{Definition.}
Reconciliation is performed per $(s,r)$. By default each scope residual is allocated
across workloads by energy share $w_{i,s,r} = E_{i,s,r}/E_{s,r}$:
\[
\text{rSCI}_i
\;=\;
\frac{1}{R_i}\sum_{s,r}\Bigl(O_{i,s,r}
\;+\; w_{i,s,r}\bigl(\Delta_{s,r}^{\text{S1}} + \Delta_{s,r}^{\text{S2}} + \Delta_{s,r}^{\text{S3}}\bigr)\Bigr),
\]
which collapses to a single per-slice intensity term:
\[
\text{rSCI}_i \;=\; \frac{1}{R_i}\sum_{s,r} E_{i,s,r}\cdot\xi_{s,r},
\quad
\xi_{s,r} = I_r + \frac{\Delta_{s,r}^{\text{S1}}+\Delta_{s,r}^{\text{S2}}+\Delta_{s,r}^{\text{S3}}}{E_{s,r}}.
\]
The only workload-specific term is $E_{i,s,r}$; $\xi_{s,r}$ is a slice-level effective
intensity that depends only on grid intensity and per-scope residuals.

\paragraph{Reconciliation by construction.}
Because $\sum_i w_{i,s,r} = 1$ within a slice, the per-workload metric sums to the
top-down report:
\[
\sum_i \text{rSCI}_i \cdot R_i \;=\; \sum_{s,r}\bigl(C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S1}} + C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S2}} + C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S3}}\bigr).
\]
Reconciliation is not a property to check empirically; it is forced by the
energy-share weighting of the residual bridges.

\paragraph{Properties.}
The energy-share weighting of the residuals closes the four gaps from the section
opening. (i) \emph{Sunk-carbon}: in \textsc{sci}, the embodied term $M$ is amortised
per server and enters \emph{per unit of work}, so it shrinks as inefficient hardware
ages. rSCI has no per-server $M$ term at all; embodied carbon lives in $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$
and is allocated \emph{per unit of energy}: $w_{i,s,r}\Delta^{\text{S3}}_{s,r}\propto E_{i,s,r}$. A
workload on older, less-efficient hardware draws more energy, takes a larger residual
share, and reports a \emph{higher} rSCI --- the opposite of the \textsc{sci} pathology.
The argument does not depend on whether the provider's $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ treats aged hardware
as fully sunk (zero remaining amortisation) or not: even when a fully-amortised server contributes
nothing to $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$, its higher per-request energy drives a higher rSCI through the
operational $E_{i,s,r}\cdot I_r$ term, which carries the per-request gradient regardless of any
embodied-amortisation choice.
(ii) \emph{Embodied incentive}: because $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ rides on the same
energy-share weighting, workloads sized to fit the actual demand --- rather than
over-reserved --- get a smaller embodied burden than under \textsc{o-sci}, which
ignores embodied entirely. (iii) \emph{Peak-shaving / procurement deferral}: at
provider scale, $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ reflects the amortised embodied carbon of the
fleet that exists to serve peak demand. A workload that contributes to peak draws
more energy at the moment when $\xi_{s,r}$ is highest and is correspondingly
penalised; \textsc{o-sci} is blind to this. (iv) \emph{Reconciliation}: the energy-share weights satisfy
$\sum_i w_{i,s,r}=1$ within each slice, so the per-workload metric sums to the
provider's top-down report as a structural identity, not an empirical check (see
\emph{Reconciliation by construction} above).

\paragraph{Real-time estimation.}
The retrospective definition requires $\Delta$ and $E_{s,r}$ from the provider's
delayed report. To use rSCI as a real-time optimisation signal we factor out grid
intensity:
\[
\xi_{s,r} \;=\; \mu_{s,r}\cdot I_r \;+\; \rho_{s,r},
\]
where $\mu_{s,r} = C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S2}}/(E_{s,r}\cdot I_r)$ is the
\emph{Scope-2 multiplier} (dimensionless; captures PUE and energy-model error) and
$\rho_{s,r} = (C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S1}}+C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S3}})/E_{s,r}$
is the \emph{non-electricity overhead intensity} (Scope~1 + 3 per Wh). Both terms are
load-independent and slow-moving; given estimates $\hat{\mu}$, $\hat{\rho}$ from the
last reconciled month,
\[
\widehat{\text{rSCI}}_i = \frac{1}{R_i}\sum_{s,r} E_{i,s,r}\bigl(\hat{\mu}_{s,r}\cdot I_r + \hat{\rho}_{s,r}\bigr),
\]
with current $I_r$. With no history, $\hat{\mu}=1$, $\hat{\rho}=0$, and
$\widehat{\text{rSCI}}$ degenerates to \textsc{o-sci} --- the framework degrades
gracefully to the prior-art operational metric when reconciliation data is
unavailable.

\paragraph{Attribution refinements --- and why Scope~3 needs a different weight.}
The energy-share weighting $w_{i,s,r}$ is the simplest legal allocation, but it is the
right default only for $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$, whose physical driver \emph{is} energy
consumed at intensity $I_r(t)$. Applied to $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ it gives the wrong
gradient. Embodied carbon is a function of \emph{what hardware exists}, which is in
turn a function of \emph{peak provisioned capacity} --- not total energy across the
period. Two pathologies follow. First, a workload that deliberately runs during a
low-CI trough still picks up an energy-proportional embodied share, even though it
consumed spare capacity that would have existed regardless of its presence; the
metric \emph{penalises} clean-grid usage on an axis the workload did not move.
Second, a workload that runs at the fleet peak draws roughly the same energy as the
trough job and is allocated the same embodied share, even though it is the workload
forcing the next server purchase. Energy-share is bookkeeping-fair, but it is not
incentive-aligned for the lever (peak shaving) that actually moves embodied.

The framework admits scope-specific weights $w^{k}_{i,s,r}$ for each residual
sub-component as long as $\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r}=1$, so reconciliation-by-construction
is preserved under any such reweighting. The principled choice for the embodied term
is a \emph{Shapley value} over the peak-capacity game: $\varphi_i$ is workload $i$'s
expected marginal contribution to fleet peak, averaged over coalition orderings.
Trough-fillers receive $\varphi_i\!\approx\!0$; peak-drivers receive $\varphi_i$
proportional to their contribution at the peak hour. Full Shapley is exponential in
the workload count, but tractable proxies retain the qualitative gradient:
(a)~\emph{peak-hour energy share} $w^{\text{S3}}_i = E_i(t^*)/E_{s,r}(t^*)$, where
$t^*$ is the slice peak hour; (b)~\emph{leave-one-out marginals}
$\text{peak}(\mathcal{N}) - \text{peak}(\mathcal{N}\setminus\{i\})$; (c)~sampled
Shapley over a few hundred coalition orderings.\todo[inline]{The Shapley/peak-share
analysis is the second-experiment subject in \S\ref{sec:practice}: synthetic toy
fleet, three allocations (energy-share / peak-hour-share / sampled Shapley),
counterfactual peak-vs-trough placement of one batch job. Expected finding: under
energy-share the embodied component is invariant to temporal shifting; under
Shapley/peak-share it drops in the trough scenario --- so the metric rewards the
shift on \emph{both} axes (clean grid + no peak contribution). Persisted from chat
2026-05-17 for a fresh-context implementation session.}
A second refinement applies to $\hat\mu_{s,r}$: state-estimation (e.g.\ Kalman
filtering across reconciled months and across pooled tenants) compresses the
operational-multiplier residual without changing the allocation. Cross-tenant
pooling exploits that $\hat\mu, \hat\rho$ are driven by provider methodology rather
than individual customers.

\paragraph{Precision scales with data fidelity.}
rSCI is a \emph{class} of metrics, not a fixed equation. Its precision is bounded by
three axes: bottom-up model quality (coverage and accuracy of $\varepsilon_p$),
top-down reporting granularity (slice resolution + cadence), and the
unit-of-work $R$. As each improves, $\Delta$ shrinks, but reconciliation holds at
any level of data fidelity. The next section confronts the theory with today's
provider reports.

\paragraph{Closing the loop.}

rSCI is a sound bottom-up/top-down bridge in theory; it cannot be computed in
practice on today's provider reports because the top-down side is too coarse, too
delayed, and methodologically opaque. The next section identifies the specific
provider, estimation, and standards changes needed to close the gap.

\section{Closing the Gap: A Call to Action}
\label{sec:asks}

% TODO: revise after experiments. Working hypothesis: the three buckets below
% (providers / estimation / standards) are the right partition; specifics may shift
% based on which axis the experiments show is most bottle-necked.

If reconciliation-by-construction is structurally available but empirically
unrealised, the question is what would change that. We see three complementary
buckets.

\paragraph{Provider asks.}
Customer carbon must be reported at \emph{billing frequency} (hourly, resource-level)
to match the rate at which workloads change. Revenue and ``equivalent revenue''
fallbacks (AWS non-foundational; cf.\ Table~\ref{tab:tier-a} footnote~$a$) should be
replaced with physical-telemetry allocation. Customer-tool methodology must reconcile
with corporate disclosure --- Microsoft's open admission that the two do not match
(\S\ref{sec:sota}) is a contract violation against the customer's reporting need, not
a feature. Excluded Scope-3 categories require published materiality assessments, as
the GHG Protocol already mandates.

\paragraph{Estimation techniques.}
Even at current reporting cadence, three lines of estimation can compress the
residual: (a) cross-tenant pooling of $\hat\mu_{s,r}, \hat\rho_{s,r}$, since these
quantities are driven by provider methodology rather than individual customers;
(b) Kalman-filter state-estimation over reconciled-month sequences to track drift in
PUE and hardware refresh; (c) Shapley-value attribution (or tractable proxies) for
the embodied term to sharpen the peak-shaving incentive beyond plain energy-share
weighting. We do not require providers to expose proprietary telemetry --- only to
report often enough that customer-side estimation can converge.

\paragraph{Standards and policy.}
The GHG Protocol is under-specified for cloud~\cite[see][]{schneider-mattia-2024};
allocation of shared infrastructure, equipment lifetimes, EAC temporal matching, and
building embodied carbon are interpretive divergences arising from gaps in the
standard, not violations~\cite[see ``Under-Specified GHG Protocol Areas'']{bashir2024}.
A cloud-specific gold standard must close these and require independent assurance of
\emph{tool implementation}, not just the methodology document.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future-work}

% TODO: tighten after §5 lands. Current draft covers (i) signals beyond carbon,
% (ii) attribution refinements, (iii) tSCI parallel, (iv) multi-tenant/serverless,
% (v) energy-coefficient standardisation.

The residual-decomposition framework is \emph{signal-agnostic}: replace
$C^{\downarrow}_{s,r}$ with a top-down water-use report and the same machinery yields
a ``water per unit of work'' metric reconcilable with the provider's water disclosure.
Scaleway and OVHcloud already publish water; abiotic-resource and waste signals could
follow. A unified attribution layer would let the community extend reconciliable
metrics to any sustainability dimension a provider chooses to report.

On the attribution side, the energy-share weighting in \S\ref{sec:rsci} is a
deliberately simple default that is only incentive-aligned for $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$.
Kalman filtering for $\hat\mu$ state-estimation and Shapley-value attribution
(or tractable proxies --- peak-hour share, leave-one-out marginals) for $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$
are the two concrete refinements we see most leverage in --- the latter sharpens the
peak-shaving / hardware-procurement incentive in a way energy-share weighting cannot,
and crucially avoids penalising clean-grid trough usage with an embodied burden it
did not cause.
\todo[inline]{Open theoretical question (chat 2026-05-17): once $\varphi_i$ is
estimated rather than computed exactly, reconciliation holds only in expectation.
Can we give a \emph{bounded-correlation} guarantee --- a worst-case bound on
$|\sum_i \widehat{\text{rSCI}}_i R_i - C^{\downarrow}_{s,r}|$ as a function of
sample budget and fleet heterogeneity? This would convert the framework's headline
property from ``reconcilable by construction'' to ``provably $\varepsilon$-close
under estimated attribution,'' which is a more honest claim under practical
attribution and likely a paper-strengthener.}

Bashir's \textsc{t-sci} and rSCI share the goal of allocating the whole datacenter
footprint to individual jobs, but tackle it from opposite directions: \textsc{t-sci}
aggregates bottom-up using datacenter telemetry only providers have; rSCI inverts
the problem and absorbs the same overhead from the top-down report. Both are sound;
rSCI is the version a customer can actually compute. A hybrid that uses \textsc{t-sci}
on the provider side as the top-down anchor for rSCI on the customer side is an
intriguing direction.

Multi-tenant and serverless settings reshape the residual decomposition in ways we
do not address here; how to allocate the residual across tenants sharing a single
warm pool is open. Finally, standardising the bottom-up energy coefficients
$\varepsilon_p$ so rSCI is comparable across organisations is the remaining
sociotechnical step.
