Data-center electricity demand is growing at an unprecedented
rate~\cite{masanet2020recalibrating,epri2024powering,bashir2024climate,gaffney2025earth},
driven primarily by generative AI. Despite the major cloud providers' 2020
pledges to reach net-zero or carbon-negative operations by
2030~\cite{google2020envreport,microsoft2020envreport,facebook2020sustainability},
recent disclosures point the other way: Google
reported a 48\% increase in GHG emissions over five
years~\cite{google2024envreport}, Microsoft a 29\%
rise since 2020~\cite{microsoft2024envreport}, and Meta a 46\% jump in 2022
alone~\cite{meta2023sustainability}. The historical
energy-efficiency gains that kept data-center electricity at a near-flat 1--2\%
share of global demand~\cite{masanet2020recalibrating} are largely
exhausted~\cite{bashir2023hotair}, and rebound (Jevons) effects are absorbing
what remains: the same chip-level efficiency improvements that made deep learning
tractable are now the engine of the demand growth~\cite{gaffney2025earth}.
Against this backdrop, regulators have begun to require firm-level GHG
disclosure --- the EU CSRD~\cite{eu2022csrd}, the SEC Climate Disclosure
Rule~\cite{SEC2024_3311275}, science-based-target
commitments~\cite{sbti2024netzero} --- which makes the accuracy and
\emph{actionability} of cloud-tenant carbon reporting a load-bearing engineering
problem, not a marketing one.

In response, a substantial body of work on \emph{carbon-aware computing} has
proposed signals to drive workload placement, scaling, and adaptation toward
lower-emission outcomes. By far the most common signal is the per-workload
\emph{operational} carbon footprint --- the product of the energy a workload
consumes and the carbon intensity of the electricity that powers it
($E\cdot I$). This single quantity drives a decade of work on temporal and
spatial load
shifting~\cite{wiesner2021letswaitawhile,radovanovic2022google,hanafy2024gaia,sukprasert2024limits,gsteiger2024caribou,murillo2024cdn,lechowicz2023pause,lechowicz2024carbonclipper},
elastic scaling~\cite{hanafy2024carbonscaler}, virtual-energy
abstractions~\cite{souza2023ecovisor}, and quality- or approximation-side
adaptation~\cite{wiesner2025qualitytime,vitali2026adaptive}; even infrastructure
planning that takes embodied carbon seriously at fleet scale still optimises
the per-workload signal against operational
energy~\cite{acun2023carbon_explorer}. The community has converged on operational
carbon not because that is the whole picture, but because it is the part that
can be computed from publicly available signals.

A more comprehensive per-workload metric was introduced in 2021 by the Green
Software Foundation: the Software Carbon Intensity (\SCI)
specification~\cite{sci-spec}, codified as ISO/IEC~21031 in 2024. \SCI extends
$E\cdot I$ with an explicit embodied term, the amortised cradle-to-gate carbon
of the hardware running the workload --- formalised by Gupta et al.'s
architectural carbon model ACT~\cite{gupta2022act} --- so that
hardware-procurement choices are reflected alongside operational energy. The
stated intent is a single per-workload number that ranges from a function call
to a datacenter, is real-time sensitive, and is meant to drive engineering
optimisation.

Though more comprehensive than operational-only signals, \SCI carries a
structural flaw: Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that flat amortisation of
embodied carbon biases \SCI toward older, less efficient hardware --- the
opposite of what total-footprint minimisation requires. Their refinements
address that bias, but a second, equally structural problem remains, shared by
\emph{every} metric in this family including the refinements themselves: none of
them connects to the cloud provider's customer-allocated GHG report. An
engineering team that improves any of these signals cannot point to a matching
change in the company's corporate sustainability disclosure, because the report
contains overheads no bottom-up energy model captures without provider-internal
data --- PUE, idle and over-provisioned capacity, market-based adjustments,
upstream fuel-and-transmission losses, building embodied carbon, refrigerant
leakage, water, and end-of-life. The Green Software Foundation explicitly treats
this gap as a feature, distinguishing ``measuring for action'' (per-workload
signals that drive engineering) from ``measuring for reporting'' (firm-level
inventories that satisfy regulators) and arguing that the two pursue different
goals and should not be conflated~\cite{gsf-measuring}. We disagree.
In an environment where regulators now require firm-level GHG
disclosure~\cite{eu2022csrd,SEC2024_3311275,sbti2024netzero} and where
carbon pricing is moving onto the cost sheet, a per-workload metric whose
improvement does not land in audited reporting will not be funded, sustained,
or trusted at scale. Microsoft's own 2021 Cloud Supply Chain whitepaper
concedes that its customer-tool methodology
``\emph{will differ from those reflected in Microsoft's corporate
disclosure}''~\cite[p.~2, fn.~2]{microsoft-s3-2021} --- a gap this paper treats
as the structural failure mode, not a design choice.

We argue that any per-workload sustainability metric intended to drive both
engineering decisions and corporate disclosure should \emph{reconcile} with the
provider's top-down GHG report by construction.
We introduce \emph{Reconcilable \SCI} (\RSCI) as a concrete metric in this
class: it anchors a bottom-up energy estimate to the provider's per-scope
top-down report through a \emph{residual bridge} for each Scope, so that
per-workload numbers sum to the reported total as a structural identity rather
than an empirical check (\S\ref{sec:rsci}). \RSCI captures all provider-side
overheads, because everything the bottom-up model misses is absorbed into the
residual rather than dropped; it is structurally sunk-carbon-free, with
embodied carbon living in the Scope-3 residual; and it degrades gracefully to
$E \cdot I$ when the provider does not yet ship reconciliation-grade data.
Critically, reconciliation forces an explicit residual term per scope, and the
choice of \emph{how} to allocate that residual across workloads becomes a
design surface that determines what the metric incentivises --- including a
peak-shaving and procurement-deferral incentive no bottom-up metric can supply
(\S\ref{sec:rsci-design}).

\section{Background: \SCI and the Sunk Carbon Fallacy}
\label{sec:background}

The community's per-workload carbon metric is the \emph{Software Carbon Intensity} (\SCI),
codified by the Green Software Foundation and adopted as ISO/IEC 21031~\cite{sci-spec}:
\[
\SCI \;=\; \frac{E \cdot I \;+\; M}{R},
\]
where $E$ is the energy consumed running the workload, $I$ the carbon intensity of that
energy, $M$ the amortised embodied carbon of the server (the device's cradle-to-gate
LCA total scaled by the request's share of its expected lifetime,
following the architectural carbon-modelling convention of Gupta et al.'s
ACT~\cite{gupta2022act}), and $R$ a unit of work
(API call, transaction, training run, $\ldots$). The metric is real-time-sensitive,
applicable from a single function to a datacenter, and intended to drive engineering
optimisation.

Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that \SCI contains a structural bug they
call the \emph{sunk-carbon fallacy}: because $M$ is amortised at a flat rate over the
device's expected lifespan, the per-unit-work $M$ does not respond to declining
efficiency, so as hardware ages, $E$ grows but $M$/$R$ does not, and a carbon-aware
scheduler optimising \SCI is biased toward older, less efficient
servers --- the opposite of what total-footprint minimisation requires. Provider
amortisation conventions (AWS/Azure 6\,yr, GCP 4\,yr; all zero out past EL ---
Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3}) sharpen this into the limit case where past-EL hardware
contributes $M=0$ per request; we note that the \SCI specification itself is silent on
$\text{TiR}>\text{EL}$, so the $M=0$ reading is a provider operationalisation rather
than an \SCI mandate, but it is the reading customers see today. To fix the underlying
fallacy, Bashir et al.\ introduce two refinements:
\[
\OSCI = \frac{E \cdot I}{R}, \qquad
\TSCI = \frac{tO + tM}{R},
\]
where \OSCI drops the embodied term entirely, and \TSCI keeps it but
allocates the \emph{entire datacenter's} operational and embodied carbon (active and
idle servers alike) across jobs by time, idle, and usage fractions. Both avoid the
sunk-carbon fallacy --- the per-job number no longer depends on the specific server it
lands on. \OSCI is cheap to compute; \TSCI is sound but demands
datacenter-wide telemetry that public-cloud customers cannot
access~\cite[\S3.1]{bashir2024}.

\OSCI resolves the sunk-carbon fallacy by dropping embodied carbon entirely,
but this is not free. Without an embodied term, \OSCI (i) does not reward
frugal hardware reservation beyond what energy savings already imply; (ii) gives no
signal to prefer hardware procured with low-embodied processes; and (iii) does not
disincentivize peak-hour use --- the lever that delays new hardware purchases in
capacity-constrained datacenters. \TSCI would restore these incentives by
construction, but, as Bashir notes, it requires comprehensive datacenter-level
information that public-cloud customers cannot
access~\cite[\S3.1]{bashir2024}.\footnote{The \TSCI ambition --- allocating the full
datacenter footprint to individual jobs --- is closely related to ours, but tackled
from the opposite direction. We return to this parallel in \S\ref{sec:future-work}.}

There is a second, structurally distinct weakness shared by every variant in the
\SCI family: none of them connects to the cloud provider's customer-allocated
GHG report. An engineering team that improves \SCI, \OSCI, or
\TSCI cannot point to a matching change in the company's corporate
sustainability disclosure. A metric whose improvement does not land in audited
reporting will not be funded, sustained, or trusted at scale --- and the cloud
provider's number does not move in response to bottom-up optimisation. The
decoupling is not merely theoretical: Jacquet et al.~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy}
demonstrate empirically that current per-VM provider attribution can
under-estimate operational CO$_2$ by up to 3$\times$ for high-utilisation
workloads on production OVHcloud servers, because static quantity-based
allocation misses the logarithmic load-dependence of server efficiency. The
next section grounds this second critique in what providers actually publish
today.

\section{State of the Art in Cloud Carbon Accounting}
\label{sec:sota}

\paragraph{Customer-facing tools are rare.}
A survey of 40+ cloud providers (May~2026) finds only eleven that ship a
first-party, per-customer carbon dashboard --- the bar for a customer to source
a GHG-Protocol-compliant Scope-3 Cat-1 input from their cloud
spend.\footnote{We exclude marketplace third-party SaaS such as Huella Cloud on
Huawei Cloud (vendor: SGS Solutions), since the per-provider allocation is not
auditable against the provider's own emission factors. Exoscale's calculator is
borderline (Exoscale-branded surface, CloudAssess engine by Resilio/Kleis) and
is omitted.} Of the remaining $\sim$30, around 13 publish corporate-level
Scope~1/2/3 disclosures with no per-customer surface (Tier~B); the rest publish
only marketing-grade renewable/PUE claims (Tier~C). Most starkly, the
fastest-growing segment --- AI/GPU clouds (CoreWeave, Lambda, Crusoe, Nebius,
RunPod, Together, Replicate, Hyperstack, DataCrunch) --- has \emph{zero}
per-customer tools. Compute capacity for AI training and inference is being
built at the fastest pace in cloud history with the \emph{least} customer
carbon transparency.

\paragraph{One shared template, four axes of variation.}
Strip the marketing and every Tier-A tool computes the same shape:
\[
\mathrm{customer\,carbon} \;=\;
\textstyle\sum_{r}\, u_{r}\,\cdot\,f_{r} \;+\; \mathrm{overheads},
\]

GB-month, \$-spend, server-hours) and $f_{r}$ is a per-unit factor. Provider
variation lives on four orthogonal axes: (A1)~which activity unit, (A2)~how
$f_{r}$ is derived, (A3)~embodied carbon treatment, (A4)~idle/overhead
allocation. Figure~\ref{fig:axes} maps the eleven Tier-A tools onto the two

taxonomy (usage-time / traffic / component-LCA) puts AWS-foundational
and OVHcloud-dedicated in different families even though both reduce to
$\sum u_{r} f_{r}$, and we replace it with the per-axis decomposition

\paragraph{The ideal weighting is measured energy --- almost no one delivers it.}
The right question is not which usage unit a provider uses but how well that
unit tracks the customer's actual energy draw. For network, bytes are
approximately linear in energy, so byte-share allocation is structurally sound
(Cloudflare, Fastly-Delivery, Akamai's byte component). For compute the
relationship is highly non-linear --- an idle server still draws on the order
of half its full-load power --- so vCPU-hours, instance-hours, and \$-spend
systematically misweight idle and busy workloads. Four customer-surface
allocations approach energy-direct, in decreasing concreteness: \emph{Scaleway}
combines per-component LCA with actual measured CPU consumption and published
Boavizta consumption profiles for the monthly report, so an idle and a
fully-loaded VM at the same SKU receive different
allocations;\footnote{\url{https://www.scaleway.com/en/docs/environmental-footprint/}; per-product calculation pages compiled in our reference set.}
\emph{Akamai} meters kWh per server and per network device in its
colocations\footnote{Akamai 2025 emissions-reporting policy and customer-reporting blog series.} and allocates DC-total energy by
``byte utilization and machine utilization'';
\emph{Oracle}'s power-based mode tracks kWh by service workload (LBM~+~MBM)
but does not disclose the shared-hardware allocation
algorithm;\footnote{\url{https://docs.oracle.com/en-us/iaas/releasenotes/governance/carbon-analysis-powercalculation.htm}, June~2025.}
and \emph{GCP-internal} measures power per machine via Borg telemetry but
deliberately re-projects to vCPU-h~$\times$~list-price at the customer
surface~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}. The rest of the Tier-A set (AWS, Azure,
IBM, Alibaba, OVHcloud-currently) propagates a usage-unit proxy: an idle and a
fully-loaded VM of the same SKU and duration produce the same number.

\paragraph{Coverage and verification are shallower than the dashboards suggest.}
Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3} breaks the Big-3 Scope-3 perimeter down by
GHG-Protocol category. No two providers cover the same set: AWS includes FERA
and full Cat~2 capital goods (with buildings); GCP adds business travel and
employee commuting but excludes end-of-life; Azure is the only Big-3 to
include Cat~12 end-of-life yet excludes both FERA and building embodied
carbon. None publishes the materiality assessment the GHG Protocol requires
to justify a category exclusion. Headline numbers are therefore not directly
comparable. Third-party assurance is similarly shallow: AWS's Apex statement

\paragraph{The internal/external gap is structural --- and is the \RSCI residual.}
Three providers say this out loud. Microsoft's 2021 Scope-3 transparency
whitepaper, attached to its own description of the customer-facing
tool, states that ``\emph{the underlying methodologies and emissions findings
generated from the calculator will differ from those reflected in
Microsoft's corporate disclosure}''~\cite[p.~2, fn.~2]{microsoft-s3-2021}, and
the January~2026 Azure methodology page repeats the point in operational terms:
``\emph{usage for emissions calculations might not match your Microsoft usage
for billing purposes}''~\cite{azure-methodology-2026}. GCP measures
machine-level power via Borg hourly but re-projects to a monthly
\emph{list-price-proportional} SKU factor at the customer surface, and
Schneider \& Mattia note (\S3.6) that ``\emph{customers do not receive a signal
about how switching from SKU~A to SKU~B can reduce their actual emissions, but
rather their allocated emissions based on list prices}''~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}.
AWS's Model~3.0 defines a foundational/non-foundational service split but
publishes neither the service list nor the share of emissions allocated by
equivalent revenue~\cite{aws-ccft-model3}. The pattern is not three isolated
quirks: every Tier-A tool ships a methodologically distinct, thinner subset of
what the provider measures internally, and the size of that gap is the
residual that \S\ref{sec:rsci} formalises through \RSCI. Bottom-up engines
such as BoaviztAPI~\cite{simon2024boaviztapi} and
Cinergy~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy} reach the same verdict from the opposite
side and are the natural feed for the bottom-up term in \RSCI; they do not
themselves reconcile to the provider report.

\section{\RSCI: Reconciling Bottom-Up and Top-Down}
\label{sec:rsci}

% NOTE: framing wrapper depends on §5 experiments. Math is committed; opening list
% of "four gaps closed" follows the §2 critique structure.

We introduce \emph{Reconcilable \SCI} (\RSCI), a class of per-workload metrics designed
to close four gaps identified in \S\ref{sec:background} and \S\ref{sec:sota}:
(i) the sunk-carbon fallacy of \SCI;
(ii) the loss of embodied-carbon incentives in \OSCI;
(iii) the absence of a peak-shaving / hardware-procurement signal in \OSCI;
(iv) the disconnect between every \SCI variant and the cloud provider's
top-down carbon report.
\RSCI does so by anchoring a bottom-up energy model to the provider's per-scope
top-down report via residual bridges, and by allocating each residual component
across workloads with a weight chosen to match its physical driver. The choice of
weight is itself a design surface --- the central vision contribution of this
paper.

\paragraph{Setup.}
Let $i$ index \emph{workload instances} --- one evaluation of a workload over period
$t_i$. The provider issues a top-down report broken into Scope-1, Scope-2, and
Scope-3 totals at the level of a \emph{reporting slice} indexed by service $s$ and
region $r$ (the finest granularity providers publish: per-month, per-project, per-service).
Let $C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S1}}$, $C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S2}}$, and
$C_{s,r}^{\downarrow\text{S3}}$ denote the reported per-scope emissions of slice $(s,r)$.

\paragraph{Bottom-up energy.}
For workload $i$ in slice $(s,r)$ we estimate energy from per-resource quantities:
\begin{equation}
E_{i,s,r} \;=\; \sum_{p} q_p \cdot \varepsilon_p \quad [\text{Wh}],
\label{eq:bu-energy}
\end{equation}
where $p$ indexes resource types (vCPU-hours, GPU-hours, GB-hours of storage, I/O ops,
network GB) and $\varepsilon_p$ is a per-type energy coefficient. Concrete
instantiations of $\varepsilon_p$ are available from open-data projects such as
BoaviztAPI~\cite{simon2024boaviztapi} (per-component LCA factors and CPU power
curves) and per-VM deterministic models such as Cinergy~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy}
(production-validated on OVHcloud); \RSCI is orthogonal to the choice of
bottom-up engine. Unobservable resource types simply enlarge the residuals
defined below.

\paragraph{Residual bridges.}
For each top-down component $k$ reported by the provider, the residual is the gap
between what the provider reports and what the bottom-up model accounts for:
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{k}_{s,r} \;=\; C^{\downarrow k}_{s,r} \;-\; O^{k}_{s,r},
\label{eq:residual-general}
\end{equation}
where $O^{k}_{s,r}$ is the bottom-up estimate of that same quantity (zero for
components the bottom-up model does not address). At the coarsest decomposition ---
one residual per scope ---
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\text{S1}}_{s,r} = C^{\downarrow\text{S1}}_{s,r}, \;\;
\Delta^{\text{S2}}_{s,r} = C^{\downarrow\text{S2}}_{s,r} - \!\sum_i\! E_{i,s,r} I_r, \;\;
\Delta^{\text{S3}}_{s,r} = C^{\downarrow\text{S3}}_{s,r},
\label{eq:residual-scopes}
\end{equation}
with $I_r$ the location-based grid intensity of region $r$.
$\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ absorbs diesel and refrigerant emissions; $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$
absorbs PUE, idle capacity, market-vs-location adjustments, and energy-model error;
$\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ absorbs embodied hardware, building embodied, FERA, upstream
transport, and the other categories of Table~\ref{tab:bigthree-s3}. Where the
provider reports finer-grained sub-categories --- and where the operator finds it
useful --- the same definition (Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-general}) admits further
splitting; we will use a minimal split of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ into FERA, embodied IT,
and non-IT (building, freight, end-of-life) below.

\paragraph{Definition.}
Reconciliation is performed per slice $(s,r)$. Each residual component $k$ is
allocated across workloads by a weight $w^{k}_{i,s,r}$ satisfying
$\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r} = 1$, and the per-workload metric is
\begin{equation}
\RSCI_i \;=\; \frac{1}{R_i}\sum_{s,r} \Bigl[\, E_{i,s,r}\cdot I_r \;+\; \sum_{k} w^{k}_{i,s,r}\cdot\Delta^{k}_{s,r} \,\Bigr].
\label{eq:rsci}
\end{equation}
The first inner term is the bottom-up operational footprint; the second is the
workload's share of each residual component under the chosen weight. The choice of
$w^{k}$ is left open by Eq.~\ref{eq:rsci}: different weight families yield metrics
that incentivise different operator behavior, while preserving reconciliation by
construction (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}). The next subsection makes this design surface
explicit.

\subsection{Weight families: the residual as a design surface}
\label{sec:rsci-design}

Three weight families cover the residual components today's provider reports
expose, each motivated by a different physical driver of the residual.

\paragraph{Energy-share weights.}
\begin{equation}
w^{k}_{i,s,r} \;=\; \frac{E_{i,s,r}}{E_{s,r}}, \qquad E_{s,r} = \sum_i E_{i,s,r}.
\label{eq:w-energy}
\end{equation}
Appropriate when the physical driver of $\Delta^{k}$ is electricity drawn: the
load-proportional portion of $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ (PUE overhead, MBM adjustments,
energy-model error), and the FERA sub-component of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ (upstream
fuel and T\&D losses are physically proportional to kWh consumed).

\paragraph{Static-share weights.}
\begin{equation}
w^{k}_{i,s,r} \;=\; \frac{1}{M_{s,r}},
\label{eq:w-static}
\end{equation}
where $M_{s,r}$ is the number of active workloads in the slice. Appropriate when the
driver is facility uptime or facility existence --- costs incurred by being ``in
the room'' rather than by consumption. Sharma and Fuerst's accountable-FaaS
framework~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless} adopts this rule for
idle power and for embodied carbon under a single-server abstraction, on the
principle (their \S5) that embodied is more naturally treated as a \emph{static sunk
cost} than a usage-based cost. We adopt the same rule for $\Delta^{\text{S1}}$
(diesel backup, refrigerant leakage) and for the non-IT sub-components of
$\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ (building embodied, freight, end-of-life).

\paragraph{Peak-attribution weights.}
\begin{equation}
w^{k}_{i,s,r} \;=\; \frac{\varphi_i}{\sum_j \varphi_j},
\label{eq:w-peak}
\end{equation}
where $\varphi_i$ is workload $i$'s contribution to fleet peak --- formally, the
Shapley value~\cite{shapley1953value} of $i$ in the cooperative game whose value
function is the slice's peak provisioned capacity. Appropriate for the embodied-IT
sub-component of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$, whose physical driver is peak provisioning:
the workload at peak forces the next server purchase; the workload at the trough
does not. The same physical argument was recently made for embodied-carbon
attribution within a single server by Han et al.~\cite{HotCarbon2024GameTheoretic},
who frame ``\emph{embodied carbon attribution as the problem of demand-driven
supply provisioning for hardware}''~\cite[\S3.4]{HotCarbon2024GameTheoretic};
\RSCI generalises the move to the slice / fleet scope via the top-down residual.
Exact Shapley over the peak-capacity game is exponential in workload count and
infeasible online; following the precedent set
by~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless,HotCarbon2024GameTheoretic},
we treat $\varphi_i$ as a \emph{best-effort Shapley approximation} satisfying the
four Shapley properties (efficiency, null-player, symmetry, linearity). Practical
proxies that retain the qualitative gradient include:
(a)~\emph{peak-hour energy share} $\varphi_i = E_i(t^{*})$ with $t^{*}$ the slice
peak hour;
(b)~\emph{leave-one-out marginals} $\varphi_i = \text{peak}(\mathcal{N}) -
\text{peak}(\mathcal{N}\setminus\{i\})$;
(c)~\emph{sampled-permutation} Shapley over a few hundred coalition
orderings~\cite{castro2009polynomial}.
The choice of proxy is itself a design surface; \RSCI reconciles under any choice
satisfying $\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r} = 1$.

\paragraph{Default mapping.}
Mapping the three families to the residual components of
Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-scopes} (and its finer Scope-3 split) yields a default schema:
energy-share for $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ and $\Delta^{\text{S3-FERA}}$; static-share
for $\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ and the non-IT sub-components of $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$;
peak-attribution for $\Delta^{\text{S3-embodied-IT}}$. This is a starting point, not
a prescription: operators with finer telemetry can split residual components further
or substitute other weights, and \RSCI reconciles as long as each chosen weight
family sums to one across workloads in the slice. The vision contribution of this
paper is the \emph{recognition that the residual is a design surface for
sustainable-behavior incentives}, not a fixed allocation rule.

\paragraph{Reconciliation by construction.}
Because $\sum_i w^{k}_{i,s,r} = 1$ for every component $k$, summing
$\RSCI_i \cdot R_i$ across all workloads in a slice yields the top-down
per-scope total:
\begin{equation}
\sum_i \RSCI_i \cdot R_i \;=\; \sum_{s,r}\Bigl(C^{\downarrow\text{S1}}_{s,r} + C^{\downarrow\text{S2}}_{s,r} + C^{\downarrow\text{S3}}_{s,r}\Bigr).
\label{eq:reconcile}
\end{equation}
Reconciliation is not an empirical property to verify; it is a structural identity
that holds under \emph{any} admissible weight choice for \emph{any} residual
component.

\paragraph{Properties.}
Under the default mapping, \RSCI closes the four gaps from the section opening as
direct consequences of the chosen weights.
(i) \emph{Sunk-carbon}: \RSCI has no per-server $M$ term. Embodied carbon lives in
$\Delta^{\text{S3-embodied-IT}}$ and is allocated by the peak-attribution weight
(Eq.~\ref{eq:w-peak}). A workload on older, less efficient hardware draws more
energy through $E_{i,s,r}\cdot I_r$ in Eq.~\ref{eq:rsci} and reports a higher \RSCI
regardless of how embodied is amortised on the provider's books --- the per-request
operational gradient carries the sunk-carbon-avoidance argument on its own.
(ii) \emph{Embodied incentive}: under static-share or peak-attribution weights for
the embodied component, workloads sized to fit actual demand pay less embodied than
over-reserved ones --- a signal \OSCI structurally cannot supply.
(iii) \emph{Peak-shaving and procurement deferral}: under peak-attribution weights
(Eq.~\ref{eq:w-peak}), a workload running at the slice peak draws a larger embodied
share than one running at the trough, \emph{even at identical energy}. This is the
incentive structure operators need to defer fleet expansion, and it is a property no
bottom-up metric (\SCI, \OSCI, \TSCI) can supply because the design surface --- a
residual to allocate --- does not exist without top-down anchoring.
(iv) \emph{Reconciliation}: Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}.

\paragraph{Negative residuals are admissible.}
Eq.~\ref{eq:residual-general} does not constrain the sign of $\Delta^{k}_{s,r}$.
When the bottom-up estimate $O^{k}_{s,r}$ exceeds the provider's reported
$C^{\downarrow k}_{s,r}$ for a component, the residual is negative and the
corresponding term in Eq.~\ref{eq:rsci} \emph{reduces} the workload's
attributed footprint. Empirically this case arises: Jacquet et
al.~\cite{jacquet2025cinergy} show that current provider attribution can
under-estimate operational CO$_2$ by up to 3$\times$ for high-utilisation VMs.
\RSCI absorbs such cases by construction --- reconciliation
(Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}) still holds --- and a negative $\Delta^{k}_{s,r}$ is
itself a diagnostic: it signals that the provider's top-down report
under-counts what a credible bottom-up model attributes to the slice. The
metric captures the top-down report; whether the top-down report captures
physical truth is the provider's accountability, not the metric's.

\paragraph{Precision scales with data fidelity.}
\RSCI is a \emph{class} of metrics parameterised by the choice of weight family per
residual component, not a fixed equation. Its precision is bounded by three axes:
bottom-up model quality (coverage and accuracy of $\varepsilon_p$), top-down
reporting granularity (slice resolution + cadence), and the unit-of-work $R$. As
each improves, $\Delta$ shrinks and the residual-allocation choice matters less;
reconciliation (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}) holds at any level of data fidelity and
under any admissible weight choice. The next section confronts the theory with
today's provider reports.

\section{Closing the Gap: A Call to Action}
\label{sec:asks}

% TODO: revise after experiments. Working hypothesis: the three buckets below
% (providers / estimation / standards) are the right partition; specifics may shift
% based on which axis the experiments show is most bottle-necked.

If reconciliation-by-construction is structurally available but empirically
unrealised, the question is what would change that. We see three complementary
buckets.

\paragraph{Provider asks.}
Customer carbon must be reported at \emph{billing frequency} (hourly, resource-level)
to match the rate at which workloads change. Revenue and ``equivalent revenue''
fallbacks (AWS non-foundational; \S\ref{sec:sota}) should be
replaced with physical-telemetry allocation. Customer-tool methodology must reconcile
with corporate disclosure --- Microsoft's open admission that the two do not match
(\S\ref{sec:sota}) is a contract violation against the customer's reporting need, not
a feature. Excluded Scope-3 categories require published materiality assessments, as
the GHG Protocol already mandates.

\paragraph{Estimation techniques.}
Even at current reporting cadence, three lines of estimation can compress the
residual: (a) cross-tenant pooling of $\hat\mu_{s,r}, \hat\rho_{s,r}$, since these
quantities are driven by provider methodology rather than individual customers;
(b) Kalman-filter state-estimation over reconciled-month sequences to track drift in
PUE and hardware refresh; (c) Shapley-value attribution (or tractable proxies) for
the embodied term to sharpen the peak-shaving incentive beyond plain energy-share
weighting. We do not require providers to expose proprietary telemetry --- only to
report often enough that customer-side estimation can converge.

\paragraph{Standards and policy.}
The GHG Protocol is under-specified for cloud~\cite[see][]{schneider-mattia-2024};
allocation of shared infrastructure, equipment lifetimes, EAC temporal matching, and
building embodied carbon are interpretive divergences arising from gaps in the
standard, not violations~\cite[see ``Under-Specified GHG Protocol Areas'']{bashir2024}.
A cloud-specific gold standard must close these and require independent assurance of
\emph{tool implementation}, not just the methodology document.

\paragraph{Beyond internal scheduling: user reporting and customer Scope-3.}
\S\ref{sec:rsci} frames \RSCI as an operator- and scheduler-facing signal, but
reconciliation by construction (Eq.~\ref{eq:reconcile}) enables two further roles
worth flagging. First, \RSCI is the only metric in the family that can be
\emph{honestly reported to end users} of a cloud-hosted service: \OSCI shows a slice
of operational electricity decoupled from the embodied, peak-driven, and overhead
costs the user's request actually triggers, whereas an \RSCI number reflects all of
these and therefore admits behavioral adaptation (load-shifting, request shaping,
volume reduction) on grounds that match what the provider will ultimately report.
A practical concern is routing-invariance --- two identical user requests should not
get different numbers because of operator placement decisions the user cannot see.
This is addressable without redefining \RSCI: because the peak-attribution weight
$\varphi_i$ depends on the workload's \emph{temporal} contribution to fleet peak
(when and how big), not on \emph{which physical machine} it lands on, the user-facing
view can marginalise over spatial placement at fixed temporal and sizing profile.
The resulting per-(request-class, submission-time) expectation is routing-invariant
for the user, preserves their temporal and sizing levers, and (by linearity of
expectation, with weights still summing to one) preserves reconciliation.
Second, the same property makes \RSCI directly usable for the customer's own
\emph{Scope-3 Cat-1} (purchased goods and services) accounting of cloud use: rather
than relying on the provider's monthly per-account total, the customer can attribute
Scope-3 across internal teams or products at workload granularity, with the
guarantee that the bottom-up breakdown sums to the provider's disclosed
total --- closing the loop a parallel bottom-up estimate cannot.

The residual-decomposition framework is \emph{signal-agnostic}: replace
$C^{\downarrow}_{s,r}$ with a top-down water-use report and the same machinery yields
a ``water per unit of work'' metric reconcilable with the provider's water disclosure.
Scaleway and OVHcloud already publish water; abiotic-resource and waste signals could
follow. A unified attribution layer would let the community extend reconciliable
metrics to any sustainability dimension a provider chooses to report.

On the attribution side, the energy-share weighting in \S\ref{sec:rsci} is a
deliberately simple default that is only incentive-aligned for $\Delta^{\text{S2}}$.
Kalman filtering for $\hat\mu$ state-estimation and Shapley-value attribution
(or tractable proxies --- peak-hour share, leave-one-out marginals) for $\Delta^{\text{S3}}$
are the two concrete refinements we see most leverage in --- the latter sharpens the
peak-shaving / hardware-procurement incentive in a way energy-share weighting cannot,
and crucially avoids penalising clean-grid trough usage with an embodied burden it
did not cause.
\todo[inline]{Open theoretical question (chat 2026-05-17): once $\varphi_i$ is
estimated rather than computed exactly, reconciliation holds only in expectation.
Can we give a \emph{bounded-correlation} guarantee --- a worst-case bound on
$|\sum_i \widehat{\RSCI}_i R_i - C^{\downarrow}_{s,r}|$ as a function of
sample budget and fleet heterogeneity? This would convert the framework's headline
property from ``reconcilable by construction'' to ``provably $\varepsilon$-close
under estimated attribution,'' which is a more honest claim under practical
attribution and likely a paper-strengthener.}

Bashir's \TSCI and \RSCI share the goal of allocating the whole datacenter
footprint to individual jobs, but tackle it from opposite directions: \TSCI
aggregates bottom-up using datacenter telemetry only providers have; \RSCI inverts
the problem and absorbs the same overhead from the top-down report. Both are sound;
\RSCI is the version a customer can actually compute. A hybrid that uses \TSCI
on the provider side as the top-down anchor for \RSCI on the customer side is an
intriguing direction.

Multi-tenant and serverless settings reshape the residual decomposition in ways we
do not address here; how to allocate the residual across tenants sharing a single
warm pool is open. Finally, standardising the bottom-up energy coefficients
$\varepsilon_p$ so \RSCI is comparable across organisations is the remaining
sociotechnical step.

\section{Cloud provider survey universe}
\label{app:inventory}

An illustrative sample of 48 cloud providers reviewed in May~2026,
clustered by focus and classified by what the provider publishes.
URLs last verified 2026-05-26.

\paragraph{Provider clusters.} Six clusters, each capturing a different point of view on the market rather than a clean
structural distinction:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item \textit{Hyperscalers}: multi-region, multi-service IaaS+PaaS+SaaS portfolios with global reach.
\item \textit{Mid-tier and regional IaaS}: general-purpose IaaS with a narrower geographic footprint or service catalog than the hyperscalers, often positioned on jurisdiction or sovereignty (e.g., EU data residency).
\item \textit{CDN / edge}: globally-distributed edge networks selling caching, request handling, and short-lived compute.
\item \textit{Neocloud}: AI-specialized compute providers with focus on GPUs and other accelerators.
\item \textit{PaaS}: developer-facing application hosting layered atop a hyperscaler. Heroku is excluded as it is being sunset under Salesforce, and broader SaaS / data-platform companies (Salesforce, Snowflake, Databricks, etc.) are out of scope for this paper.
\item \textit{Sustainability-positioned regionals}: regional IaaS or DC operators whose primary differentiator is environmental, e.g. Nordic siting, heat reuse, hydro-powered grids.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Status taxonomy.} Five mutually-exclusive disclosure clusters,
collapsing into the three tiers used in \S\ref{sec:sota}:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
\item \statusA{Per-customer tool} (Tier A): first-party dashboard or API exposing workload-level emissions per tenant.
\item \statusB{Assured GHG report} (Tier B): Scope~1/2/3 corporate inventory with external assurance --- SBTi-validated, third-party-audited, CSRD-baselined, or ISO~14064-certified. The corporate number is reliable enough to inherit downstream as Scope~3.
\item \statusB{Self-reported} (Tier B): published Scope~1/2/3 inventory, \emph{or} substantive quantitative operational disclosure (per-DC carbon intensity, PUE, energy mix, EMAS environmental statement), without external GHG assurance.
\item \statusC{Marketing claim} (Tier C): forward net-zero pledge, ``100\,\% renewable'' / REC-backed statement, or qualitative sustainability strategy, but no quantified disclosure.
\item \statusC{No disclosure} (Tier C): nothing surfaced through our search.
\end{itemize}
