\begin{abstract}
Organizations seeking to reduce the carbon footprint of their cloud applications must rely on two largely disconnected ways of measuring it, each with important limitations.
Bottom-up metrics like Software Carbon Intensity (\SCI) provide real-time signals for carbon-aware optimization but are blind to most provider-side overheads.
Top-down reports by cloud providers provide a more comprehensive view of a tenant's carbon footprint but are coarse and methodologically opaque.
Because the two approaches differ in scope and reporting frequency, the current state of the art treats them as decoupled: bottom-up metrics for optimization, top-down reports for corporate disclosure.

We argue that the two signals should be \emph{reconcilable}. 
A per-work\-load metric whose improvements never surface in the provider's audited report will not be adopted for accountability at scale.
We survey the state of the art in cloud carbon accounting and propose a vision for reconciled \SCI (\RSCI): a per-workload metric that re-anchors a bottom-up energy estimate to the cloud provider's top-down report through a residual.
By decomposing and allocating this residual according to its physical drivers, \RSCI can preserve operational incentives while also attributing idle capacity and embodied carbon to the workloads that drive them.
We show that today's top-down reporting is still too coarse and methodologically inconsistent to support credible reconciliation and lay out concrete steps in provider reporting and standards to make it practical.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A major challenge in sustainable computing is quantifying the carbon footprint of workloads. 
It is a prerequisite for determining the effectiveness of carbon-reduction strategies, for reporting to regulators and investors, and for making informed decisions about workload scheduling and adaptation. 
Two largely separate approaches exist: bottom-up modeling and top-down corporate greenhouse gas (GHG) reporting. % based on provider-wide measurements and downstream allocation to tenants.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.95\columnwidth]{figures/carbon_accounting_tension.pdf}
  \caption{A provider's top-down carbon report and a tenant's bottom-up estimate rarely match. We call for \emph{reconciling} the two totals and allocating the residual back to workloads.}
\end{figure}

\parag{Bottom-up metrics}
Most carbon-aware computing research has focused on optimizing bottom-up metrics like operational carbon: the estimated emissions from a workload's marginal increase in energy consumption. 
This signal is straightforward to obtain (workload energy $\times$ carbon intensity) but does not, for example, capture the embodied carbon of the hardware running the workload.
To provide a more comprehensive metric, the Green Software Foundation proposed the Software Carbon Intensity (\SCI) specification~\cite{sci-spec}, codified as ISO/IEC~21031 in 2024, which expresses the carbon emitted per unit of work and folds in an embodied term.
However, Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that \SCI can produce perverse incentives for workload placement and scaling in practice. 
Furthermore, every bottom-up metric, \SCI included, can only capture what a tenant can observe or model, and is blind to most provider-side overheads.

\parag{Top-down reporting}
Due to regulatory pressure, cloud providers increasingly offer customer-facing dashboards and reports enabling tenants to estimate and disclose their cloud carbon footprint in line with the GHG Protocol~\cite{ghg-protocol-corp}.
While the reporting boundaries and allocation methods can differ significantly by provider, the general methodology is usually public and sometimes even externally audited.
However, the reported numbers are highly aggregated: 
reports mostly contain a single number per month, region, customer, and GHG Protocol scope, sometimes coarsely categorized further into service types like ``Compute'' or ``Storage''.
The reports are published about 14-21 days after the end of each month, which makes them comprehensive but unsuitable for any type of real-time optimization.

\parag{The need for reconcilability}
Today, when an engineering team improves the bottom-up metric of an internal workload, it is entirely unclear whether that improvement reaches the provider's top-down report.
The two can diverge in both directions.
The top-down report contains overheads that bottom-up models cannot cover without internal provider data.
And conversely, the actions a bottom-up metric does capture, such as lowering a VM's utilization and thus its power draw, may not move the reported number at all, if the provider allocates carbon to tenants by usage-hours or revenue instead of measured energy.

The Green Software Foundation explicitly endorses this separation, distinguishing ``measuring for action'' (per-workload signals that drive engineering) from ``measuring for reporting'' (organization-level inventories that satisfy regulators) and arguing that the two serve different goals and should not be conflated~\cite{gsf-measuring}.
\emph{We disagree.}
Now that regulators require organization-level GHG disclosure~\cite{eu2022csrd,SEC2024_3311275,sbti2024netzero} and carbon pricing is increasingly implemented, organizations need workload-level signals that can attribute their reported cloud emissions across internal teams, services, and products. 
A per-workload metric whose gains never land in official reporting will not be trusted and adopted for accountability at scale.

\parag{Contributions}
We argue that bottom-up carbon metrics should \emph{reconcile} with the provider's top-down GHG report by construction.
Towards this, we make the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
\item We review the state of the art in cloud carbon accounting, including a survey of 48 providers and a matrix showing which engineering actions actually move which provider's reported number.
\item  We introduce \emph{reconciled Software Carbon Intensity} (\RSCI), which anchors a bottom-up energy estimate to the provider's top-down report through a \emph{residual}, so per-workload numbers sum to the reported total by construction.
\item We decompose the residual into energy-driven and capacity-driven components, so \RSCI can account not only for the energy a workload consumes, but also for the idle capacity and embodied hardware it contributes to.
\item We diagnose why today's provider reports are too coarse and methodologically opaque for computing credible \RSCI scores in practice, and set out concrete asks on reporting and standards.
\end{itemize}

\section{Bottom-Up Metrics Miss Provider Overheads}
\label{sec:background}

A bottom-up metric estimates a workload's carbon from quantities a tenant can measure without access to providers' internal data.
The de facto choice in most research papers is operational carbon, which we write as \OSCI (operational software carbon intensity) to match the terminology in Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024}:
\[
\OSCI \;=\; \frac{\E \cdot \I}{\R},
\]
the workload's energy $\E$ times its region's carbon intensity $\I$ (in gCO$_2$/kWh), per functional unit of work $\R$ (an API call, a training run, a GB stored per month).\footnote{We write CO$_2$ or ``carbon'' as shorthand for CO$_2$-equivalent emissions throughout.}
It is straightforward, transparent, and it responds to the two operational dimensions a tenant controls directly: energy efficiency and carbon-aware timing or placement.
But it says nothing about hardware efficiency, i.e., whether the workload requires less provisioned capacity.

To capture this missing dimension, the Software Carbon Intensity (\SCI) specification (ISO/IEC~21031~\cite{sci-spec}) adds an embodied term:
\[
\SCI \;=\; \frac{\E \cdot \I \;+\; \M}{\R},
\]
where $\M$ is the workload's amortized share of the hardware's embodied carbon, allocated according to its reserved time and resource share as described by Gupta et al.~\cite{gupta2022act}.
The specification defines three categories of \emph{action} for reducing software carbon: (i) energy efficiency, (ii) hardware efficiency, and (iii) carbon awareness, and requires the score to fall whenever a tenant takes any of these actions~\cite{sci-spec}.
\OSCI already responds to the first and third through \E and \I, the embodied term \M is what extends \SCI to the second.

However, Bashir et al.~\cite{bashir2024} show that \SCI is subject to a \emph{sunk-carbon fallacy}: because \M is amortized flat over the hardware's expected lifespan, \M/\R does not reflect declining relative hardware efficiency. 
As older hardware becomes less efficient relative to newer alternatives, its already-amortized embodied component can make it appear artificially attractive, so a scheduler minimizing \SCI can drift toward older hardware and increase total emissions.
They therefore recommend sticking to \OSCI for operational decisions such as scheduling and scaling. 
But \OSCI is incomplete: it rewards neither reserving less hardware (beyond the energy it saves), nor purchasing less and lower-embodied hardware.

Most importantly, independent of the embodied term, \emph{bottom-up-only metrics are structurally blind to provider-side overheads}, such as idle and over-provisioned capacity.
\SCI acknowledges this (``It is a score rather than a total''~\cite{sci-spec}), but the implication is that a tenant can improve its \SCI or \OSCI without moving the audited number.
We argue that a metric that cannot be reconciled to the audited report will not be adopted for accountability at scale.

\section{Top-Down Reports Respond Inconsistently to Carbon-Reduction Actions}
\label{sec:sota}

Under regulatory pressure~\cite{eu2022csrd,SEC2024_3311275,sbti2024netzero}, cloud providers have moved beyond annual corporate reports toward customer-facing tools that attribute emissions to individual tenants.
But a top-down report is only useful for engineering if it responds to the same actions a per-workload metric rewards.
We surveyed 48 cloud providers in May~2026, spanning hyperscalers, regional IaaS, CDN and edge networks, GPU-focused neoclouds, application-hosting PaaS, and sustainability-positioned regionals.
Twenty-six publish some form of quantitative emissions disclosure, whether self-reported or externally audited, but only twelve ship a first-party, per-customer tool, and just nine of these meter reservable compute in enough detail to trace a workload.
None of the eleven GPU neoclouds we surveyed expose per-tenant emissions, even as AI training and inference concentrate there.
Table~\ref{tab:levers} records, for each, whether the three actions, defined in \S\ref {sec:background}, move the reported number.
No provider responds to all actions, and the same action can be reflected inconsistently across providers’ reports.

This is because a top-down report is built in three steps: providers decide \emph{what} emissions to count, \emph{allocate} the datacenter total down to services and tenants, and \emph{report} the result at a chosen granularity.
Each step decides which engineering actions still move the reported number.
We now walk through each step in more detail.

\parag{What cloud providers cover}
Providers decompose their total datacenter emissions over the reporting period along the GHG Protocol's three scopes.
\emph{Scope~1}, direct on-site emissions such as backup-diesel combustion and refrigerant leakage, is usually negligible for most tenants today, though the increasing deployment of behind-the-meter gas generation at datacenters may change this in future.
\emph{Scope~2} is the datacenter's purchased electricity valued at the region's grid carbon intensity.\footnote{The GHG Protocol mandates dual reporting for Scope 2: \emph{location-based} figures reflect actual grid emissions, while \emph{market-based} figures net out procurement instruments like power purchase agreements (PPAs) and renewable energy certificates (RECs), which in practice often moves the market-based number to near zero. Since engineering teams have no control over the provider's procurement choices, the market-based figure is not actionable for workload optimization. which is why \SCI explicitly excludes it~\cite{sci-spec}. We only consider the location-based figure in this paper.}
\emph{Scope~3} covers amortized cradle-to-gate hardware and, depending on the provider, building embodied carbon, upstream fuel-and-transmission losses (FERA), freight, and end-of-life.
No two of the three hyperscalers count the same set of Scope-3 categories.
For example, only GCP counts employee business travel and commuting~\cite{google-carbon-methodology}, only Azure counts hardware end-of-life~\cite{microsoft-s3-2021}, and building-embodied carbon is counted by AWS and GCP but not Azure~\cite{aws-ccft-model3,google-carbon-methodology}.
So even the headline totals are not directly comparable.

\parag{How cloud providers allocate}
Each provider then allocates the datacenter total to tenants in proportion to their share of an \emph{activity unit}.
The units are inconsistent and lean on economics to differing degrees.
AWS runs two regimes at once, physical instance-hours for services with dedicated racks, \emph{equivalent revenue} for the rest, without publishing which service falls on which side~\cite{aws-ccft-model3}.
GCP hides an economic weighting inside a physical-looking unit: it distributes each product's energy across SKUs in proportion to list price before allocating by per-SKU usage such as vCPU-hours~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}.

None of the hyperscalers allocates by \emph{measured} energy, which is certainly hard to do in virtualized infrastructure at scale.
As a result, two identical VMs of the same type and duration, one idle and one fully loaded, are attributed the same emissions (Table~\ref{tab:levers}, \emph{lower util.}\ column).
Smaller providers show this is not fundamental: Oracle's power-based mode, Scaleway, and T~Cloud allocate compute by measured energy, so an idle and a busy instance diverge, making the action individually feasible, just not unified in any one tool.
The activity unit also decides the \emph{efficient-resource} action: switching to a more efficient instance lowers the number only where the per-unit factor is grounded in energy or LCA, whereas at GCP it tracks list price, so a cheaper-but-dirtier SKU can look greener~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}.

\parag{How cloud providers report}
Customer dashboards report at the service level (for GCP, ``Compute Engine,'' ``Networking,'' \ldots), far coarser than a bottom-up metric's per-workload view: a service spans dozens of SKUs and a SKU thousands of workloads, and none of that structure survives into what is published.
A tenant sees Scope 1, 2, and 3 totals per service $\times$ region $\times$ month, released 14 to 21 days after the period closes.
This monthly cadence erases \emph{when} a tenant runs: AWS and Azure apply a monthly-average carbon intensity, and although GCP computes on hourly carbon intensity internally, it collapses it into a fleet-wide per-SKU-and-region average before allocating by monthly usage~\cite{schneider-mattia-2024}, so shifting the same work to cleaner hours changes nothing (Table~\ref{tab:levers}, \emph{time} column).
Providers also differ on \emph{how} Scope~2 is valued: AWS, GCP, and most others expose a location-based figure that reflects the regional grid, so a cleaner region lowers the number. Azure and T~Cloud export only the near-zero market-based figure, hiding the region action entirely.

\section{Reconciling Bottom-Up and Top-Down}
\label{sec:rsci}

We introduce \emph{reconcilable \SCI} (\RSCI), a family of per-workload metrics that anchors bottom-up carbon estimates to a provider's top-down report. We decompose the residual (the gap between top-down and bottom-up) into energy-driven components, which scale with the bottom-up estimates, and capacity-driven components, which we allocate to workloads to incentivise peak-shaving. \RSCI scores sum to the reported total by construction, making every per-workload improvement traceable to the audited total.

\parag{Setup}
Let $i$ index a \emph{workload}: a distinct billable unit a tenant runs, like a service or a batch job.
A reporting \emph{slice} is the provider's finest published granularity (service $\times$ region $\times$ month).
A workload can span multiple slices (e.g., multiple services or regions).
Rewriting \OSCI and \SCI using this per-workload notation we get
\begin{equation}
\OSCI_i \;=\; \frac{\sum_s \E_{i,s}\cdot\I_s}{\R_i}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\SCI_i \;=\; \frac{\sum_s \E_{i,s}\cdot\I_s + \M_i}{\R_i},
\label{eq:osci-sci-repetition}
\end{equation}
where $\E_{i,s}$ is the workload's energy within the slice, $\I_s$ the slice's location-based carbon intensity, $\M_i$ the workload machine's embodied term, and $\R_i$ the functional unit totalled over the period.

\OSCI, \SCI, and \RSCI all rely on a bottom-up energy estimate $\E_{i,s}$.
We can, for example, derive $\E_{i,s}$ from per-resource quantities,
\begin{equation}
\E_{i,s} \;=\; \mathrm{PUE}\cdot\sum_{p} q_{i,s,p} \cdot \varepsilon_p,
\label{eq:bu-energy}
\end{equation}
where $p$ indexes resources (vCPU-hours, GPU-hours, GB-hours of storage, I/O ops, network GB), 
$q_{i,s,p}$ is workload $i$'s measured consumption of resource $p$ within slice $s$,
$\varepsilon_p$ is a per-type energy coefficient from engines such as BoaviztAPI~\cite{simon2024boaviztapi}.
$\mathrm{PUE}$ (power usage effectiveness) is an optional factor for modeling facility overhead.

\parag{Reconciliation}
A bottom-up model cannot capture all energy usage within Scope 2: coefficients are approximate, idle energy cannot be modeled, and a fixed PUE ignores that real facility overhead varies with season and weather.
Emissions in Scope 1 and 3 are even less observable.
Thereform, for each slice, we define the \emph{residual} emissions as the difference between the provider's top-down reported total carbon footprint $C^{\downarrow}_{s}$ and the bottom-up estimate:
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{s} \;=\; C^{\downarrow}_{s} \;-\; \sum_i \E_{i,s}\,\I_s .
\label{eq:residual-total}
\end{equation}
\RSCI distributes this residual across the slice's workloads by weights.
It uses the same operational term as \OSCI, but adds a residual term, taken over one or more \emph{residual components} $\Delta^{c}_s$ (with $\sum_c \Delta^{c}_s = \Delta_s$), each distributed across the slice's workloads by a weight $w^{c}_{i,s}$ that sums to one ($\sum_{i \in s} w^{c}_{i,s} = 1$):
\begin{equation}
\RSCI_i \;=\; \frac{\sum_s \bigl( \overbrace{\E_{i,s}\cdot\I_s}^\text{operational term} \;+\; \overbrace{\textstyle\sum_c w^{c}_{i,s}\,\Delta^{c}_{s}}^\text{residual term}\;\;\;\bigr)}{\R_i}.
\label{eq:rsci}
\end{equation}
Attributed carbon then reconciles by construction: scaling each score back to carbon by its $\R_i$, the slice's \RSCI values sum to the reported total,
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\textstyle\sum_i \RSCI_i\,\R_i
&=
\textstyle\sum_s
\left(
\sum_{i \in s} \E_{i,s}\I_s
+
\sum_c \Delta^c_s \sum_{i \in s} w^c_{i,s}
\right) \\
&=
\textstyle\sum_s
\left(
\sum_{i \in s} \E_{i,s}\I_s
+
\Delta_s
\right)
=
\textstyle\sum_s C^\downarrow_s .
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:reconcile}
\end{equation}
A workload spanning several slices sums its per-slice attributions, and since each balances on its own, reconciliation composes to the fleet total.

This identity holds for \emph{any} partition of the residual into components and \emph{any} normalized weights.
Both are free, and different choices incentivise different behavior.


\parag{Decomposing the residual}
The simplest allocation choice would be to split the full residual $\Delta_s$ evenly, giving each of the slice's $N_s$ workloads a share $w_i = 1/N_s$.
This reconciles, but creates no incentives.
To improve this, we first decompose the residual the way the provider reports it, by GHG scope.
Let $C_{s}^{\downarrow\text{S1}}$, $C_{s}^{\downarrow\text{S2}}$, $C_{s}^{\downarrow\text{S3}}$ denote the top-down reported per-slice, per-scope emissions.
Since our bottom-up model estimates only operational Scope~2 carbon, the per-scope residuals (summing to $\Delta_s$) are
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\text{S1}}_{s} = C^{\downarrow\text{S1}}_{s}, \qquad
\Delta^{\text{S2}}_{s} = C^{\downarrow\text{S2}}_{s} - \sum_i \E_{i,s}\,\I_s, \qquad
\Delta^{\text{S3}}_{s} = C^{\downarrow\text{S3}}_{s},
\label{eq:residual-scopes}
\end{equation}
$\Delta^{\text{S1}}$ absorbs diesel and refrigerants;
$\Delta^{\text{S2}}$ idle capacity and energy-model error;
$\Delta^{\text{S3}}$ embodied hardware and the rest of Scope~3.

The scope split mirrors the provider's reporting but not the actions a tenant controls.
What matters for incentives is whether an emission scales with \emph{energy usage} (i.e., the bottom-up model) or with \emph{provisioned capacity} (i.e., idle power and embodied emissions).
We therefore propose regrouping the three scope residuals into an energy-driven $\Delta^{\text{en}}_{s}$ and a capacity-driven $\Delta^{\text{cap}}_{s}$ term, writing $\beta^{k}\in[0,1]$ for the energy-driven fraction of scope $k$'s residual:
\begin{equation}
\Delta^{\text{en}}_{s} = \sum_{k} \beta^{k}\,\Delta^{k}_{s}, \qquad
\Delta^{\text{cap}}_{s} = \sum_{k} (1-\beta^{k})\,\Delta^{k}_{s}.
\label{eq:residual-levers}
\end{equation}
The betas must be estimated by tenants.
However, they only tune the incentive structure, so rough estimates suffice:
Scope~1 carries no energy-driven part ($\beta^{\text{S1}}=0$).
Scope~2 mixes energy-driven terms (unmodeled overhead, energy-model error) with capacity-driven ones (idle power), so $\beta^{\text{S2}}$ depends on how well the bottom-up model captures the energy-driven part.
Scope~3 is mostly capacity-driven (embodied hardware and buildings), with a smaller energy-driven part (electricity upstream, i.e.\ FERA).

Since $\Delta^{\text{en}}_s + \Delta^{\text{cap}}_s = \Delta_s$, any $\beta$ preserves reconciliation. 

\parag{Allocating the residual}
The energy-driven residual $\Delta^{\text{en}}$ should scale proportionally with the operational term.
We attribute by,
\begin{equation}
w^{\text{en}}_{i,s} = \frac{\E_{i,s}\cdot\I_s}{\sum_{j \in s} \E_{j,s}\cdot\I_s} = \frac{\E_{i,s}}{\sum_{j \in s} \E_{j,s}}.
\label{eq:w-energy}
\end{equation}
Within a slice, where carbon intensity is fixed, this allocates the energy-driven residual in proportion to modeled energy use. If finer-grained time-varying carbon intensity is available, the same rule can be applied over time-indexed sub-slices, so that consumption during dirtier periods receives a larger attribution.

The capacity-driven residual $\Delta^{\text{cap}}$ is not a direct cost of running a workload but it must still be allocated.
Sharma and Fuerst split such shared costs evenly across workloads~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless}.
We instead attribute by peak capacity, following Shapley-based accounting for shared datacentre power~\cite{islam2016energyaccounting} and the peak-provisioning argument of Han et al.~\cite{HotCarbon2024GameTheoretic}: the workloads running during peak times drive the next server purchase.
The residual then takes the weight
\begin{equation}
w^{\text{cap}}_{i,s} = \frac{\varphi_i}{\sum_j \varphi_j} ,
\label{eq:w-peak}
\end{equation}

where $\varphi_i$ is workload $i$'s Shapley share~\cite{shapley1953value} of the period's peak capacity. 
Computing exact Shapley is expensive, but practical proxies (e.g., peak-hour energy share or sampled permutations) provide estimates that preserve the intended incentive structure.

\parag{Online use of \RSCI}
Exact \RSCI scores are retrospective as the residual $\Delta_s$ is only known once the provider publishes its top-down report, and the capacity weight $\varphi_i$ depends on the realized load profile over the reporting period. 
During the period, tenants can still optimize against provisional scores that estimate residuals from historical reports and approximate capacity weights from online signals such as peak-hour energy share or forecast peak contribution. 
These scores are then adjusted to match the final \RSCI values once the report arrives.
This is analogous to peak-demand electricity tariffs, where consumers optimize against signals such as peak periods before their final bill is calculated~\cite{bartusch2011demandtariff}.
Developing and evaluating online estimators that are accurate enough for control and explicit about uncertainty is future work.


\section{Example}
\label{sec:demo}

To illustrate \RSCI's reconciliation and incentives, we work one example end to end: we model a single workload on a single node, reporting carbon per token served. Results are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:demo}.

\parag{Scenario}
An organization reserves a bare-metal 8$\times$H100 node to serve an internal LLM over a three-day window and wants to report an \SCI-like metric: gCO$_2$ per million tokens served (gCO$_2$/Mtok), for each hour of the window.
The node draws about $2.5$\,kW idle and up to $10.2$\,kW at full load~\cite{nvidia-dgx-h100-datasheet} and we assume a PUE of $1.2$.
We model load using the 2024 Azure LLM inference trace~\cite{stojkovic-dynamollm-2025} and carbon intensity using a trace for the California grid (CAISO) provided by Electricity Maps~\cite{electricitymaps2026}.
Retrospectively, the provider reports the node's footprint top-down: $C^{\downarrow\text{S2}}\!=\!80$\,kg CO$_2$ for Scope~2 and $C^{\downarrow\text{S3}}\!=\!20$\,kg for Scope~3 (we omit Scope~1 for simplicity).
We ask how well the aggregated carbon-per-token metrics reconcile to that $100$\,kg total, and what behaviour they reward along the way.

\parag{\OSCI}
This operational metric counts only the marginal energy the work draws above idle, scaled by the PUE and the hourly carbon intensity.
It rewards the main actions a tenant controls in this scenario: using less energy (\E) and computing work during cleaner hours (\I).
By construction the node's idle power and its embodied hardware are not considered.
Its advantage over \SCI is that, lacking any embodied term, it cannot be misguided by depreciated hardware (i.e., the sunk-carbon fallacy~\cite{bashir2024}).
\OSCI reports between 52 and 270\,gCO$_2$/Mtok (${\sim}5.2\times$ range) and the aggregated scores reach about 58\,kg.

\parag{\SCI}
\SCI adds an amortized embodied term of \M $\approx 14$\,gCO$_2$/Mtok.\footnote{We take the node's cradle-to-gate embodied carbon as ${\approx}2500$\,kg: NVIDIA reports $1312$\,kg for the 8-GPU HGX~H100 baseboard~\cite{nvidia2025hgxh100pcf}, plus CPUs, memory, storage, NICs, power supplies, and cooling. We assume the node is amortised over a 4-year lifespan, leading to \M $\approx 14$ gCO$_2$ per million token served .}
This prices embodied carbon, but the term is only about a tenth of the operational signal, so the aggregate barely rises above \OSCI and still falls short of the provider's $100$\,kg report.

\parag{\RSCI}
\RSCI keeps \OSCI's operational term and adds the residual, the gap between the provider's reported total and the bottom-up estimate.
Following \S\ref{sec:rsci}, we split the residual into an energy-driven part, $\Delta_s^{\text{en}}\!\approx\!13$\,kg (upstream electricity and model error), and a capacity-driven part, $\Delta_s^{\text{cap}}\!\approx\!39$\,kg (idle and embodied). 
The split is controlled by provider-specific $\beta$ values.\footnote{We assume $\beta^{\text{S2}}\!=\!0.1$, as the Scope~2 gap is caused mostly by idle energy, and $\beta^{\text{S3}}\!=\!0.5$, as Scope~3 divides between FERA (upstream electricity) and embodied emissions.}

By construction, \RSCI's carbon-per-token scores sum up to the reported $100$\,kg.
The energy-driven residual is allocated in proportion to bottom-up modeled energy demand, adding 11--57\,gCO$_2$/Mtok (${\sim}5.2\times$ range).
The capacity-driven residual is allocated by peak-hour attribution, a practical online proxy for Shapley-based accounting, contributing a larger 13--142\,gCO$_2$/Mtok (${\sim}11\times$ range). 
The exact magnitudes are illustrative, but the split shows the intended incentive: \RSCI still rewards short-term operational improvements such as lower energy use or cleaner hours, while making peak demand visible enough to prioritize peak-shaving when it dominates the reconciled footprint.

\parag{Comparison}
Only \RSCI satisfies all five properties in Table~\ref{tab:properties}:

\begin{itemize}
\setlength{\itemsep}{2pt}
  \item \emph{Rewards operational actions.} Its operational term responds to the same operational actions as \OSCI and \SCI.
  \item \emph{Prices embodied carbon.} Past hardware purchases are a sunk cost and should not steer operational choices~\cite{bashir2024}, but ignoring embodied carbon understates a team's true footprint and drops its incentive to provision less hardware.
  \item \emph{Avoids the sunk-carbon fallacy~\cite{bashir2024}.} As embodied never enters a per-server \M, a workload on older hardware gets no benefit, it simply draws more energy and reports a higher \RSCI.
  \item \emph{Prices peak demand.} Hardware is provisioned for peak load, so requests at peak account for more provisioned capacity. \RSCI rewards tenants who flatten or shift demand, aiming to reduce \emph{future} hardware needs and embodied carbon.
  \item \emph{Reconciles by construction.} \RSCI is the only metric that lets per-workload scores sum to the provider's reported total.
\end{itemize}

As discussed at the end of \S\ref{sec:rsci}, a tradeoff of \RSCI compared to bottom-up-only metrics is that \emph{final scores are retrospective}: precise residuals and capacity-driven weights are only known after the reporting period.

\section{Barriers to Practical Adoption}
\label{sec:practice}

We previously argued that a per-workload carbon metric is suitable for accounting only if it can be reconciled to the provider's audited report. \RSCI satisfies this requirement by construction: the attributed workload scores sum to the provider's reported total. However, reconciliation alone is not sufficient for trustworthy accounting. In practice, computing credible \RSCI scores requires provider reports that are sufficiently detailed, transparent, and standardized.

To evaluate whether this is possible today, we attempted to compute \RSCI scores using production accounts on AWS and Azure, as well as test deployments on Scaleway. 
We identify three barriers that prevent practical adoption today: provider reports are too coarse to support workload attribution, the reported totals are insufficiently transparent to explain how they were derived, and providers lack a common reporting standard, making reconciliation inconsistent across platforms.

\parag{Top-down reports are too coarse}
\RSCI's per-workload attribution is straightforward when a reporting slice contains a single workload, as in \S\ref{sec:demo}. 
In practice, however, providers aggregate many workloads into a small number of monthly service categories.
The coarser the report, across both resources and time, the more attribution depends on heuristic allocation and requires assumptions that are not supported by the reported data

We observed this on six months of production data from an AWS account running a web service.
Although AWS bills individual services separately, its carbon report aggregates most emissions into a service category called ``Other'', see Figure~\ref{fig:aws}. 
Across the six months, this category accounts for 67--96\,\% of reported emissions and combines diverse services such as load balancing, data transfer, control-plane operations, and public IPv4 addresses. 
Because these service categories aggregate over very different usage patterns, there is no principled way to attribute the reported emissions back to individual workloads.

\begin{callforaction}
Providers already meter and bill customer usage at fine granularity.
Customer-facing carbon reporting should preserve this and publish emissions per billable resource and reporting period.
This would reduce heuristic attribution and make reconciliation substantially more credible.
\end{callforaction}

\parag{Reported totals are opaque}
\todo{highlight the uncorrelated plots}
Even at sufficient granularity, a reconciled metric can only anchor to a provider's reported total if that total is itself trustworthy.
Today, customer-facing reports are difficult to reproduce or interpret, making it impossible to distinguish modeling error from reporting methodology.

We found this even for AWS EC2, whose methodology is comparatively well documented.
Using standard bottom-up energy models, our estimated emissions exceeded the reported values by roughly 2$\times$ at idle and 5$\times$ at moderate utilization, producing a negative residual.
A negative residual is admissable mathematically, but in general we always expect a bottom-up model to underestimate the top-down report.
The residual is impossible to interpret: either our model overestimates energy, the provider underestimates emissions, or the allocation methodology differs in undocumented ways.

Other providers exhibit similar issues.
For example, Scaleway is one of the few providers that reports \emph{daily} carbon emissions, but we found that these daily totals changed when we queried them a week later, and occasionally reported past emissions before the user account was created.
Microsoft reports stable monthly reports, but explicitly notes that customer-facing emissions ``will differ from those reflected in Microsoft's corporate disclosure''~\cite{microsoft-s3-2021}.
Without stable, reproducible totals, reconciliation cannot provide trustworthy workload attribution.



\begin{callforaction}
Customer-facing carbon reports should be reproducible and auditable.
Providers should disclose enough intermediate information and methodology for tenants to independently explain reported totals and distinguish reporting assumptions from modeling error.
\end{callforaction}

\parag{There is no common standard}
As we lined out in \S\ref{sec:sota}, providers differ in what they report, how they allocate it, and what they count.
Azure and T~Cloud expose only market-based Scope~2 emissions, leaving no location-based figure to reconcile against.
AWS allocates much of its footprint by spend, GCP incorporates list-price weighting, while Oracle, Scaleway, and T~Cloud allocate compute from measured energy.
Even the hyperscalers disagree on Scope~3 boundaries, including different combinations of upstream fuel losses, buildings, end-of-life, and business travel.

These differences ultimately reflect gaps in today's GHG Protocol guidance for cloud infrastructure, which leaves shared-infrastructure allocation, equipment lifetimes, and several reporting boundaries largely to provider discretion.
\RSCI depends only on the structure of top-down reporting rather than any specific methodology, so improvements to the standard only strengthen the metric.

\begin{callforaction}
The GHG Protocol should define cloud-specific guidance for customer-facing reporting, including common reporting boundaries, allocation rules for shared infrastructure, and standardized outputs.
A standard would make provider reports comparable and enable \RSCI to be portable across clouds.
\end{callforaction}


\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:future}

In this paper, we proposed \RSCI, a per-workload metric that anchors a bottom-up energy estimate to the provider's top-down report through a residual, so that per-workload scores sum to the audited total by construction.
We showed how to decompose and allocate that residual to reward peak-shaving, and found that today's provider reports remain too coarse, opaque, and inconsistent across providers to compute credible \RSCI scores in practice.

\parag{Toward a real-time signal}
As defined, \RSCI is a \emph{retrospective} metric: the residuals $\Delta_s$ and the weight denominators ($\E_s$, the slice peak) are known only once the provider's report lands, weeks to months late.
Its native use is therefore \emph{accounting} (faithful allocation of an audited total), and its incentives bite at reporting cadence, like a carbon chargeback, rather than in a closed scheduling loop.
Turning it into a real-time signal is an open problem: predict each residual component ahead of the report.
This is plausible precisely because a good bottom-up model leaves a residual dominated by \emph{slow-moving, methodology-driven} overheads (PUE, embodied intensity, allocation constants) rather than workload dynamics, so it should be learnable from the sequence of reconciled months.
No single per-Wh multiplier captures this under the per-component schema, since different components ride different drivers (electricity, fleet existence, peak share) and need separate estimators; but each is a low-dimensional, slowly-varying quantity well suited to online state estimation, cross-tenant pooling of shared overheads, and Kalman-filter-style updating (Sharma and Fuerst apply exactly this class of filter to online \emph{marginal-energy} profiling of serverless functions~\cite{sharma2024accountable_footprint_serverless}; here it would be lifted from energy to the residual).
Absent any history the estimators default to zero residual and \RSCI degrades gracefully to \OSCI, the prior-art operational metric.
